## 1. Page identity

God reveals himself by name. Not as an abstraction, not as a philosophical category, but by name — and each name is given in a specific historical moment, at a specific place, in response to a specific situation. The student learns Genesis by learning Elohim at creation. The student learns Exodus by learning YHWH at the burning bush, YHWH-Rapha at Marah, YHWH-Nissi at Rephidim. The student learns the Psalms by learning YHWH-Ro'i under David's pen. The student learns the end of the exile by learning YHWH-Shammah in Ezekiel's final vision.

The page presents fourteen names along a revelation timeline. Each name anchored to its first-revelation verse. The timeline ends — not anticlimactically with Malachi, but with Jesus' seven "I AM" statements in John, because the New Testament's claim is that the man who said `egō eimi` was the one who said `ehyeh asher ehyeh` at the bush. The Hebrew-to-Greek continuity is the page's NT capstone.

Tone: reverent, cumulative, discoverable. A page where each click deepens.

## 3. Depth 2 framework sections

### Section 1 — The Tetragrammaton (YHWH) as covenant name

- **Ex 3:13–15** — Moses asks the name; God answers `ehyeh asher ehyeh` ("I AM THAT I AM") and names himself YHWH.
- **Ex 6:2–3** — "I appeared unto Abraham, unto Isaac, and unto Jacob, by the name of God Almighty (El Shaddai), but by my name YHWH was I not known to them." The covenant-name's distinctive revelation to Moses's generation.
- Pronunciation / vocalization: the Masoretic Hebrew preserves consonants only; Jewish tradition substitutes Adonai at reading; the reconstructed vocalization "Yahweh" is scholarly inference, not textual certainty. Surface honestly.
- **Why the Tetragrammaton matters structurally:** this is the name around which the compound names cluster. Every compound (Jireh, Rapha, Nissi, etc.) is "YHWH + attribute." The compounds are contextual revelations of the covenant name under specific circumstances.

### Section 2 — Compound YHWH names as contextual revelations

Each compound reveals God in the situation where the name is given.

- **YHWH-Jireh** (Gen 22:14) — "the LORD will provide." Given at Moriah after the ram substitution.
- **YHWH-Rapha** (Ex 15:26) — "the LORD that heals thee." Given at Marah after the bitter waters were sweetened.
- **YHWH-Nissi** (Ex 17:15) — "the LORD my banner." Given at Rephidim after the Amalek battle with Moses's hands lifted.
- **YHWH-Shalom** (Jdg 6:24) — "the LORD is peace." Given to Gideon at Ophrah after the angel's visitation.
- **YHWH-Ro'i** (Ps 23:1) — "the LORD is my shepherd." David's confessional name.
- **YHWH-Tsidkenu** (Jer 23:6) — "the LORD our righteousness." Given in Jeremiah's oracle of the Branch from David.
- **YHWH-Shammah** (Ezek 48:35) — "the LORD is there." The final name of the restored city in Ezekiel's vision.

### Section 3 — El-compounds as attribute names

- **Elohim** (Gen 1:1) — the plural-of-majesty Creator name. First name of Scripture.
- **El Shaddai** (Gen 17:1) — "God Almighty." Given to Abram at covenant-renewal.
- **El Elyon** (Gen 14:18–22) — "God Most High." Melchizedek's priestly name.
- **El Olam** (Gen 21:33) — "the Everlasting God." Abraham's planting at Beersheba.

### Section 4 — YHWH Sabaoth and Adonai

- **YHWH Sabaoth / Tzeva'ot** (1 Sam 1:3; pervasive in prophets) — "the LORD of hosts." Military / angelic-host connotation. The name most frequent in Isaiah.
- **Adonai** (Gen 15:2) — "my Lord / Master." The vocalization substituted for YHWH in Jewish reading practice; also used independently as a reverent address.

### Section 5 — Translation history

- **Hebrew YHWH** → **Greek Kyrios** (Septuagint, 3rd–2nd c. BC)
- **Greek Kyrios** → **Latin Dominus** (Vulgate, Jerome 4th c. AD)
- **Latin Dominus** → **English LORD** (Tyndale tradition → KJV 1611; small-caps convention preserves the YHWH reading)
- The translation chain flattens the Tetragrammaton into a title (Lord / LORD) rather than a name. The small-caps convention in English Bibles is the visible signal that "LORD" in that verse represents the Tetragrammaton.
- Why this matters for study: when a student reads "the LORD" in capitals in Ex 3 or Ps 23, they are reading the Tetragrammaton. The NT's `Kyrios Iēsous` ("Jesus is Lord") in Rom 10:9 is no accident — it carries the Septuagintal weight.

### Section 6 — Jesus' "I AM" statements as NT capstone

- **Seven predicated "I AM"s in John** — 6:35 (bread), 8:12 (light), 10:7/9 (door), 10:11/14 (good shepherd), 11:25 (resurrection and life), 14:6 (way, truth, life), 15:1/5 (true vine).
- **Absolute "I AM"s** — 8:24, 8:28, 8:58, 13:19, 18:5–8 (where the arresting party falls backward).
- **Claim-of-deity structure** — the continuity of Hebrew `ehyeh` / Greek `egō eimi` is the linguistic bridge. Jesus' Jewish audience at John 8:58 responded by picking up stones — they heard the claim.
- This section cross-links to `/title/i-am` (shipped Batch 7-B) where the full treatment lives. The Names of God page surfaces the capstone as a bridge; `/title/i-am` carries the depth.

## 10. Known contested points

1. **Count of names is contested.** Fourteen is one defensible selection (3 general names + 3 El-compounds + 7 YHWH-compounds + 1 Sabaoth). Bullinger's *Companion Bible* appendix lists more. Some traditions count only the seven YHWH-compounds. The site's fourteen is editorial — if Pastor Marc wants a different count (7 Jehovah compounds only, or a 21-name expanded list), override before F3 fires.

2. **Pronunciation of YHWH** is scholarly reconstruction, not textual certainty. The site uses "YHWH" (consonantal Tetragrammaton) and notes "Yahweh" as the scholarly reconstruction. "Jehovah" is the 16th-century Latin transliteration using YHWH consonants with Adonai vowel-points — it's a hybrid, not a historical pronunciation. Surface honestly.

3. **El Shaddai etymology is disputed.** "Almighty" is the traditional translation; modern Hebraists have proposed "mountain-God" (from *shad* = breast / mountain) or "destroyer" (from *shadad*). Surface the disputes; note the traditional reading anchors the commentary tradition.

4. **Ex 6:3 apparent tension with prior YHWH usage.** The verse says YHWH was not known to the patriarchs by that name, yet Gen 12–50 uses YHWH freely. Classical resolution: "known" means "experienced in covenant-faithfulness," not "unfamiliar as a term." Surface via Henry / Calvin (both PD).

5. **Whether the compound YHWH-names are technical divine names or descriptive phrases.** Hebrew grammar doesn't sharply distinguish. The translation convention of treating "Jireh," "Rapha," etc., as names (as opposed to verbal phrases "the LORD provides," "the LORD heals") is interpretive. Surface via Gesenius.
