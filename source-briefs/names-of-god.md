## Page intent

The names of God in scripture aren't synonyms. They're revelations. Each name discloses something about who God is — sovereignty, presence, provision, mercy, judgment, holiness — and God reveals new names progressively, at moments of crisis, covenant, or worship.

The page traces fourteen Old Testament names of God in the chronological order they were first revealed in scripture, plus three New Testament extensions in which Jesus and the apostles apply those names to Christ. A student walking the page experiences God's self-disclosure as it actually happened — Genesis to Revelation, Elohim to Lord — not as a flat catalog but as a story arc.

The aesthetic thesis: a vertical revelation timeline as the depth-1 visual. Each name occupies its position at the moment of first revelation. The student scrolls down through redemptive history, watching God reveal himself one name at a time.

The page's editorial posture: every name reveals; none contains. God names himself for our sake — so we can address him, trust him, call on him — but the named God is always larger than the name. The "I will be what I will be" rendering of YHWH at depth 2.2 is the page's central acknowledgment of this paradox.

Distinct from Strong's Concordance (a comprehensive lexical reference): this page is a curated front door to the most theologically loaded names, organized for revelation and worship rather than lexical completeness.

## Depth 2 — Framework

Five framework sections.

### 2.1 Three foundational names — Elohim, YHWH, Adonai

The three most-used names of God in the OT carry the whole vocabulary of divine self-reference. Every other name is built from or paired with these three.

**Elohim** — appears 2,600+ times. From the root meaning "to be strong" or "the powerful one." Plural in form (the "-im" ending) but consistently takes singular verbs, a grammatical anomaly classical commentators have read as the *plural of majesty* (a single being so great that singular form cannot contain him) and which Christian theology has read as accommodating the doctrine of the Trinity from the very first verse of scripture: *"In the beginning, God [Elohim] created…"*

**YHWH** — the Tetragrammaton (Greek for "four letters"). Appears 6,800+ times — more than any other name. The personal covenant name God reveals to Moses at the burning bush. By the Second Temple period, Jewish reverence for the name had grown so intense that it was no longer pronounced aloud; the High Priest spoke it once a year on the Day of Atonement, and Adonai or Hashem ("the Name") was substituted in synagogue reading. The original vowel pointing is uncertain. "Yahweh" is the modern scholarly reconstruction; "Jehovah" is a medieval Latinization that combined YHWH's consonants with Adonai's vowels.

**Adonai** — Lord, Master. ~439 occurrences. Distinct from "Lord" with capital-L lowercase letters (which translates YHWH); Adonai is the original Hebrew word for Lord/Master. In the Masoretic Text, when the rabbis wanted to signal "say Adonai aloud here even though the text reads YHWH," they pointed YHWH with Adonai's vowels — the source of the medieval "Jehovah."

Card cluster — three cards, one per foundational name. Each card carries the Hebrew letterform large, the transliteration, the meaning, the count, and a one-paragraph explanation.

### 2.2 YHWH — the name that means "I will be what I will be"

Featured spotlight section. The single most important grammatical and theological note on the entire page.

**The standard translation:**

In Exodus 3:14, when Moses asks God's name, God answers: *"I AM THAT I AM."* This is the King James rendering, and it's the rendering most English readers know. It identifies God as eternal being — the One who simply *is*, the self-existent, dependent on nothing.

That translation is correct. It is not the only correct translation.

**The Hebrew imperfect tense:**

The Hebrew verb form God uses — *ehyeh asher ehyeh* (אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה) — is in the *imperfect* tense. Hebrew imperfect doesn't lock cleanly to past, present, or future the way English tenses do. It denotes ongoing, incomplete, unfolding action. The same verb form can be legitimately translated in any of these ways:

- **"I AM THAT I AM"** — eternal being (KJV, traditional)
- **"I WILL BE WHAT I WILL BE"** — future, unfinished, always becoming
- **"I AM BECOMING WHAT I AM BECOMING"** — continuous self-disclosure

All three are grammatically defensible. The Hebrew refuses to choose between them. The name itself encodes a refusal to be locked into a single tense.

**What this means:**

God's name to Moses is not a static definition; it is a verb in motion. He is not just *who he was* (the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — which Moses already knew). He is *who he will be* — ongoing, unfolding, future-directed. To Israel about to be delivered from Egypt, the name is a promise: *whatever you need me to be, I will be that. Whatever the next moment requires, I will fill it.*

**Why this fits the page's posture:**

Every other name on this page reveals a specific aspect of God — Provider, Healer, Banner, Peace, Shepherd, Righteousness. The name YHWH revealed at the burning bush refuses that specificity. It says: *I am all of these and more, and I will be more still, and you cannot finish defining me by listing my names.*

This is the page's central tension and its central honesty. **Every name reveals. None contains.** The names are real, true, given for our worship and our trust. But the One who bears them is always larger than the sum of the names.

**Why Israel stopped saying it:**

The growing Jewish reluctance to pronounce YHWH was not superstition. It was reverence for a name that, on the giver's own grammar, refused to be limited to anything we could say about it. To utter the name as if we knew what it meant was, in some sense, to make it smaller than it is. Saying *Adonai* or *Hashem* in its place was a confession: this name is bigger than my mouth can carry.

**Closing framing:**

The God of Israel has a personal name. He gave it freely. And the name itself, in its own grammar, prevents us from ever thinking we have understood God simply because we know what to call him.

Featured commentary voice: **John Gill**, *Exposition of the Bible* on Exodus 3 — particularly thorough on the Hebrew grammatical analysis and the tense ambiguity of *ehyeh*.

Pastor Marc editorial hook: this is the page's strongest single ground for founder voice. The grammatical-and-theological observation here — that God's name is a verb in motion — is the kind of insight that earns student trust in Hebrew scripture's depth.

### 2.4 The "I AM" capstone — Jesus claims the name

Featured spotlight section. The page's strongest New Testament integration. The OT names of God are not retired in the NT; they are *applied to Jesus.*

**The seven "I AM" statements in John:**

Jesus uses the Greek phrase *egō eimi* ("I am") in seven structured self-revelations in John's gospel, deliberately echoing YHWH's *ehyeh* at the burning bush:

1. **"I am the bread of life"** (John 6:35)
2. **"I am the light of the world"** (John 8:12)
3. **"I am the door"** (John 10:9)
4. **"I am the good shepherd"** (John 10:11) — echoes YHWH Ra'ah
5. **"I am the resurrection and the life"** (John 11:25)
6. **"I am the way, the truth, and the life"** (John 14:6)
7. **"I am the true vine"** (John 15:1)

Each statement applies a specific aspect of YHWH's character — Provider (bread), Light, Access (door), Shepherd, Life, Truth, Vine — to Jesus himself.

**The bare "I AM" — John 8:58:**

The most dramatic moment is unadorned. In John 8:58, Jesus says: *"Before Abraham was, I am."* No predicate. No metaphor. Just the bare *egō eimi* — the Greek translation the Septuagint uses for YHWH's *ehyeh* at the burning bush.

The Jewish leaders' response makes the meaning unmistakable: *"Then took they up stones to cast at him."* (John 8:59) Stoning was the prescribed punishment for blasphemy. They understood Jesus to be claiming the divine name — applying YHWH's self-disclosure to himself.

**The arrest scene — John 18:6:**

When the soldiers come to arrest Jesus in Gethsemane and ask for "Jesus of Nazareth," Jesus answers *egō eimi* — "I am." John records: *"As soon then as he had said unto them, I am he, they went backward, and fell to the ground."* The same divine name, spoken at the moment of his self-surrender, knocks the arrest party flat.

**The OT names applied to Christ:**

The pattern extends across the NT:
- **Lord (Kyrios)** — the Greek translation of both YHWH and Adonai is applied to Jesus throughout the NT (Phil 2:9–11, Rom 10:9, 1 Cor 12:3)
- **YHWH Tsidkenu** — Jeremiah's "the LORD our righteousness" is fulfilled in Christ (1 Cor 1:30: *"Christ Jesus… is made unto us… righteousness"*)
- **YHWH Ra'ah** — the LORD my shepherd is fulfilled in the Good Shepherd (John 10:11; Heb 13:20)
- **YHWH Shammah** — the LORD is there culminates in the New Jerusalem where God dwells with humanity (Rev 21:3 — *"Behold, the tabernacle of God is with men, and he will dwell with them"*; cross-link to Tabernacle §2.4 cube continuity)
- **El Elyon** — God Most High echoed in Luke 1:32 (Gabriel to Mary: *"He shall be called the Son of the Highest [Elyon]"*)

**Closing framing:**

Every name God revealed across the Old Testament finds its embodiment in Christ. The progressive revelation that begins with Elohim at creation and unfolds through fourteen names ends with a man on a hillside in Galilee saying *"Before Abraham was, I am"* — and the religious establishment reaching for stones because they understood exactly what name he had just claimed.

### 2.5 The names that are not names — Hashem, Adoshem, the unspoken name

Brief framework card on the Jewish reverence tradition that grew up around the divine name.

**Hashem** (הַשֵּׁם) — literally "the Name." Used in modern Jewish discourse and in the Masoretic Text where YHWH appears but is not to be pronounced. Saying "Hashem" is a way of referring to the name without uttering it.

**Adoshem** — a contraction of Adonai and Hashem, sometimes used as a further substitute.

**The Tetragrammaton tradition:** by the late Second Temple period, YHWH was no longer spoken in everyday use. The High Priest pronounced it once a year on the Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur, behind the veil in the Holy of Holies). After the Temple's destruction in AD 70, even that single annual utterance ceased. The original pronunciation was effectively lost — preserved only in scholarly reconstructions and in occasional transliterations in Greek and other early Christian sources.

**Why this matters for the page's posture:**

The Jewish reluctance to pronounce God's name is not a footnote; it is part of the page's argument. A name that the One who bears it admits is larger than any tense ("I will be what I will be") becomes, in the worship of his people, a name too large for casual speech. The reverence grows organically from the grammar.

This card is short — one card, one paragraph plus the Hebrew terms with letterforms — but it earns its place as the page's acknowledgment that even the names we have are received with care.
