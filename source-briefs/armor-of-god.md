## 1. Page identity

Paul writes Ephesians from house arrest, chained to a Roman soldier (Acts 28:16, 28:30). When he tells the Ephesian believers to "put on the whole armor of God," he is describing the equipment of the man he is physically shackled to. The imagery is not abstract; it is as concrete as the soldier's pilum leaning in the corner.

But Paul is doing something more than borrowing a military metaphor. Every piece of armor he names is equipment that the Old Testament attributes to YHWH himself. Isaiah 59:17 — the anchor passage — describes YHWH putting on righteousness as a breastplate and salvation as a helmet to execute judgment. Isaiah 11:5 belts the Branch with righteousness and faithfulness. Isaiah 52:7 and Nah 1:15 shod the herald's feet with the gospel of peace. Ps 91 + Gen 15:1 make YHWH the shield. Isaiah 49:2 + Heb 4:12 make the Word the sword.

The armor Paul tells the believer to wear is the armor YHWH wears. The defense is not borrowed equipment; it is participation in divine equipment.

The page surfaces this OT echo-structure with an echo-badge on every piece at depth 1. The Roman-military background grounds the imagery historically (Vegetius PD source). The theological load-bearing is the Isaiah 59 anchor.

Tone: plainspoken, practical, cumulative. A page a student could come back to weekly.

## 3. Depth 2 framework sections

### Section 1 — Roman military background

- Paul writes Ephesians (and Philippians, Colossians, Philemon) from Roman imprisonment, most plausibly AD 60–62, chained to a rotating Praetorian guard (Acts 28:16, 28:30; Phil 1:13).
- The equipment Paul describes corresponds to 1st c. AD Imperial Roman legionary / Praetorian equipment. The vocabulary choices are specific:
  - `thureos` (v. 16) = the *scutum*, the large rectangular body-shield (not `aspis`, the smaller round Greek shield)
  - `machaira` (v. 17) = the *gladius*, the short two-edged stabbing sword (not `rhomphaia`, the longer curved blade)
  - `thōrax` = the torso armor (lorica hamata chain or lorica segmentata plate, both extant in this period)
- Source: Vegetius, *De re militari* (4th c. AD, PD Latin text; English translations PD via 19th c. Clarke or earlier).
- Note for accuracy: Vegetius writes later than Paul but describes traditions extending back into the Imperial army's classical form. For strictly contemporaneous equipment references, Josephus' *Wars of the Jews* (Whiston 1737 PD) contains detailed descriptions of the Roman legion Titus brought against Jerusalem in AD 70.

### Section 2 — The Isaiah 59:17 anchor

- **Isa 59:17** — "For he put on righteousness as a breastplate, and an helmet of salvation upon his head; and he put on the garments of vengeance for clothing, and was clad with zeal as a cloke."
- The passage describes YHWH dressing himself for judgment-execution against injustice (context: Isa 59:1–16 describes Israel's moral failure and God's deciding to act himself because no intercessor is found).
- **Paul's redeploy:** the same equipment Isaiah says YHWH wears for judgment, Paul tells the Ephesian church to wear for spiritual combat. The theological implication: the church is participating in the eschatological judgment-work of God, not merely defending itself with God-given tools.
- Two pieces in Eph 6 specifically trace to Isa 59:17: breastplate of righteousness, helmet of salvation. The direct verbal echo is unmistakable.

### Section 3 — Each piece's OT echo surveyed

- **Belt of truth** ↔ Isa 11:5 — "And righteousness shall be the girdle of his loins, and faithfulness the girdle of his reins." The Branch from Jesse (= messianic) belted with righteousness-and-faithfulness. Paul collapses both qualities into *alētheia* (truth).
- **Breastplate of righteousness** ↔ Isa 59:17 — direct echo.
- **Feet shod with gospel of peace** ↔ Isa 52:7 / Nah 1:15 — "How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that publisheth peace." The herald-runner imagery. Paul applies the herald's footing-stance to every believer.
- **Shield of faith** ↔ Ps 91:4 ("his truth shall be thy shield and buckler") and Gen 15:1 ("I am thy shield, and thy exceeding great reward"). YHWH himself is the shield; the believer's faith receives the shield that is God's own faithfulness.
- **Helmet of salvation** ↔ Isa 59:17 — direct echo. Also 1 Thess 5:8 ("and for an helmet, the hope of salvation") is Paul's own earlier redeployment; Eph 6 is the fuller treatment.
- **Sword of the Spirit** ↔ Isa 49:2 ("he hath made my mouth like a sharp sword") and Heb 4:12 ("the word of God is quick, and powerful, and sharper than any twoedged sword"). The Servant's mouth is a sword. The believer receives the same sword via the Spirit.

### Section 4 — Prayer as posture (Eph 6:18)

- The armor section does not end at v. 17 with the sword; it extends through v. 18: "Praying always with all prayer and supplication in the Spirit, and watching thereunto with all perseverance and supplication for all saints."
- Prayer is not a seventh armor piece. Prayer is the *stance* from which the armor is worn.
- Structural argument: the armor without prayer is equipment without stance; the stance without armor is posture without substance. Paul holds them inseparable.
- Contrast: some popular treatments of Eph 6 enumerate "seven pieces of armor" including prayer. The text's grammar doesn't support that enumeration — prayer is participially linked to the active verb "to stand" (v. 14), not added as a seventh item. Surface the grammatical care.

3. **`feet-shod-gospel-of-peace`** — Eph 6:15 + Isa 52:7 + Nah 1:15 in full. Herald-runner typology. Roman equivalent: *caligae* (hobnailed sandals, Vegetius + Josephus). The footing-stance metaphor.

4. **`shield-of-faith`** — Eph 6:16 + Ps 91 + Gen 15:1 in full. *Thureos* as scutum, the testudo formation as collective-defense illustration (Josephus citation on Roman testudo against Jerusalem walls). Faith as reception of divine-shield-ness.

5. **`helmet-of-salvation`** — Eph 6:17 + Isa 59:17 + 1 Thess 5:8 in full. The double-redeployment (Paul used the same image earlier). Salvation as hope-framing-of-mind.

7. **`prayer-as-posture`** — Eph 6:18 in full. The participial-grammar exposition (why prayer is posture, not seventh armor). "Watching thereunto with all perseverance" — the vigilance imagery. Cross-link to any future prayer-theology feature page.

## 10. Known contested points

1. **Prayer as armor piece or posture.** Popular treatments sometimes enumerate "seven pieces of armor" including prayer. The text's participial grammar supports "six pieces + prayer-as-stance" more naturally. Site defaults to the grammatical reading; flags the popular alternative.

2. **Imputed vs. practical righteousness** (breastplate). Reformed reads the breastplate as Christ's imputed righteousness; Wesleyan reads it as the believer's practical holiness worked out. Surface both with §7.9 care.

3. **Sword as Scripture or sword as Spirit-speaking.** `Rhēma` ("spoken word") vs. `logos` ("Word / Scripture whole"). The Pentecostal tradition reads `rhēma` emphasizing Spirit-inspired specific utterance; the Reformed tradition reads the sword as Scripture objectively. Both are defensible; surface both.

4. **Roman-military period accuracy.** Which specific equipment Paul saw matters for accurate visual rendering. 1st c. AD Imperial Praetorian equipment is the safest target. Avoid Late Roman / Early Medieval conflations.

5. **Vegetius' anachronism caveat.** Vegetius writes ~400 AD looking back; for direct Paul-contemporaneous equipment description, Josephus is closer. Cite both; note the dating.
