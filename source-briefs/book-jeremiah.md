## 1. Book identity

Jeremiah is the weeping prophet, called in Judah's final generation to announce the judgment he could not prevent and the covenant he could only glimpse. His ministry spans roughly 627 BC (call under Josiah, Jer 1) through the fall of Jerusalem (586 BC, Jer 39 / 52) and into Egyptian exile (Jer 43–44). Across 52 chapters he laments, confronts false prophets, symbolic-acts-the-coming-siege (buying a field, breaking a pot, wearing a yoke), announces a 70-year exile with a return to follow, and — at the book's theological apex (Jer 31:31–34) — reveals the New Covenant: a day coming when God writes his law on hearts rather than tablets.

The book is long, partly unchronological, mixes prose and poetry, and carries some of Scripture's heaviest personal lament alongside its most hope-shaped covenant promise. Students often find it difficult because the chapter order doesn't follow chronology and the rhetorical mode shifts frequently. The hub page's job is to give them the shape before they start reading.

Tone: sober, textually grounded, carefully sourced. The Branch Christology (Jer 23:5–6, 33:15–16) and New Covenant passage are load-bearing for NT exposition — many verses on this page cross-surface into Wave F feature pages.

## 4. Stat strip

- **Author:** Jeremiah, son of Hilkiah, of the priestly family of Anathoth (Jer 1:1). Scribe Baruch assisted with dictation (Jer 36:4, 45:1).
- **Approximate date of composition:** ministry 627–580 BC; book compiled during/after exile.
- **Audience:** Judah in its final generation; exilic and post-exilic readers.
- **Theme (one line):** "The weeping prophet of the New Covenant."
- **Chapter count:** 52.
- **Distinguishing note:** not chronological; mixes prose and poetry; contains dated oracles (many chapters begin with a king's reign reference).

## 5. Depth 2 framework sections (book hub framework)

1. **Ministry overview and historical setting** — 627 BC call under Josiah → Jehoiakim's burning of the scroll (Jer 36) → Zedekiah's siege → Jerusalem's fall (Jer 39 / 52) → Egyptian exile (Jer 43–44). Political context: Babylonian ascendancy, Egyptian client-state politics, prophetic voice against court-prophet flattery.
2. **The New Covenant (Jer 31:31–34)** — the book's theological apex. Surveyed here with cross-link to `/featured/covenants/new`. Heb 8's extended quotation anchors the NT reception.
3. **The Branch (Jer 23:5–6 and 33:15–16) and YHWH Tsidkenu** — messianic prophecy of the righteous Branch, with YHWH Tsidkenu ("the LORD our righteousness") as the name given to the coming king. Cross-link to `/featured/names-of-god/yhwh-tsidkenu`.
4. **False prophets and true prophecy (Jer 23, 27, 28)** — Jeremiah's polemic against Hananiah and others; the test of prophetic authenticity; the weight of speaking in the LORD's name.
5. **Symbolic actions and personal lament** — the buried loincloth (Jer 13), the potter's house (Jer 18), the broken pot (Jer 19), the yoke (Jer 27–28), the purchased field (Jer 32). Paired with the personal confessions (Jer 11:18–23, 12:1–6, 15:10–21, 17:14–18, 18:19–23, 20:7–18).
6. **70-year prophecy and return (Jer 25:11–12, 29:10)** — the explicit 70-year figure; Daniel's later calculation from this (Dan 9); the return under Cyrus (cross-link to Ezra once shipped in Wave D).

## 6. Chapter summary guidance (for the 52-chapter AI draft queue)

Each chapter summary: 2–4 sentences, informative, non-confessional, anchored to the chapter's primary content. For Jeremiah specifically:

- **Note dated-oracle pattern** — many chapters begin with a king's reign reference (Josiah / Jehoiakim / Zedekiah); include the dating when present.
- **Note prose vs poetry mode** — Jeremiah shifts frequently; note which mode dominates when it affects reading.
- **Flag symbolic-action chapters** — readers need to know when a chapter is primarily a symbolic act (e.g., Jer 13, 19, 32) rather than an oracle.
- **Flag the personal-confession passages** — the "confessions of Jeremiah" (listed in Section 5 item 5) carry distinct rhetorical weight.
- **Flag cross-surface anchor chapters** — Jer 23 (Branch), Jer 31 (new covenant), Jer 32 (the field at Anathoth) — summaries should be crisp on why these chapters matter beyond their local content.
- **Avoid eschatological adjudication** — Jer 30–33 (the "book of consolation") has varied millennial readings. Summary presents the text; doesn't adjudicate.

## 10. Known contested points (surface honestly)

1. **70-year counting.** Jer 25:11–12 and 29:10 both give 70 years, but the start-date is debated: 605 BC (first exile under Nebuchadnezzar, Jer 25 dating) → 538 BC (Cyrus decree) gives 67 years; 586 BC (Jerusalem fall) → 516 BC (Temple rebuilt) gives 70 years. Both readings are defensible in the tradition. Present without adjudicating.
2. **Branch Christology timing.** Whether Jer 23:5–6 is fulfilled in Christ's first coming, second coming, or progressively (per Vision §4.6 tri-fold taxonomy) is interpretively contested. Reformed readings vary; dispensational readings push more toward second-coming fulfillment. Surface without picking.
3. **New Covenant application.** Whether the New Covenant applies fully to the church now (standard Reformed covenant-theology reading) or awaits Israel's future national restoration (dispensational reading) is one of the major contemporary intra-Christian divides. §7.9 framework discipline: present both, site does not adjudicate, Pastor Marc's editorial drawer may take a position.
4. **Chapter chronology.** Jeremiah's chapters aren't in strict chronological order; some scholars propose reconstructions, others argue the canonical order has its own literary logic. Note the non-chronology on the hub; don't reorder for students.
5. **Baruch's role.** Whether Baruch (Jer 36:4) composed the book's final form or Jeremiah did is a standard critical question. Traditional view: Jeremiah authored, Baruch scribed. Note without adjudicating.
