## 1. Book identity

2 John is one of the shortest books in the NT — a single-chapter personal letter of thirteen verses from "the elder" (1:1) to "the elect lady and her children" (1:1). The traditional author is John the apostle; "the elder" is the same self-designation found in 3 John 1:1, linking both as late-life letters from the same figure who wrote the Gospel and 1 John. Date: c. 85–95 AD, from Ephesus in traditional reading.

The letter's brevity does not undercut its theological concern. John writes because a specific danger has appeared: traveling teachers who "confess not that Jesus Christ is come in the flesh" (1:7) — Docetic denial of the genuine Incarnation. Such teachers should not be received into homes or greeted (1:10–11), which in first-century practice meant cutting off the hospitality-patronage that sustained traveling-teacher ministry. The instruction is severe and has generated ongoing hermeneutical debate about how it applies.

Central theme: love and truth are inseparable. "This is love, that we walk after his commandments" (1:6) — love is not an affect independent of obedience; the commandment is the "old commandment which we had from the beginning" (1:5), reaching back to Jesus's own instruction (John 13:34, the "new commandment" that is, paradoxically, old).

## 2. Signature verse rationale

2 John 1:6 is the letter's definitional statement on love: that love is walking in the commandments. Short, complete, memorable. Captures the whole letter's ethic in one line.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** "the elder" — traditional: John the apostle. Same author as 3 John and (by near-universal Christian tradition) 1 John and the Gospel of John.
- **Date:** c. 85–95 AD
- **Audience:** "the elect lady and her children" — debated between a literal woman and her family vs. a church (personified) and its members
- **Theme:** love, truth, and warning against those who deny the Incarnation
- **Distinguishing notes:** one of the shortest NT books (13 verses); single chapter; personal-letter genre

## 9. Known contested points

- **"Elect lady" identity.** (a) Literal Christian woman and her biological children, known to John; (b) metaphor for a local church ("lady" as figurative for a church, "children" for its members). Strong PD commentary for both. Surface; don't adjudicate.
- **"The elder" identity.** Traditional: John the apostle. Some patristic traditions (esp. Papias via Eusebius *HE* 3.39) distinguish between "John the apostle" and "John the elder" as two separate figures. Majority Christian tradition: same person. Note briefly.
