## 1. Book identity

Zechariah prophesies alongside Haggai in post-exilic Jerusalem (520–518 BC for chs 1–8; later for chs 9–14), partnering directly in the Temple-rebuilding effort (Ezra 5:1, 6:14). The book divides naturally into two halves of very different character.

Chapters 1–8 are framed around eight night visions (chs 1–6) — horses among myrtle trees, four horns and four craftsmen, a measuring line, the high priest Joshua clothed, the golden lampstand, the flying scroll, the woman in an ephah, the four chariots — each interpreted for Zechariah by an angel. The visions address the returned community's immediate questions: will God restore? Is the rebuilt Temple legitimate? Will the Davidic line survive? Two symbolic acts close this section: crowning Joshua the high priest (ch 6, "the man whose name is The Branch") and speeches on fasting and restoration (chs 7–8).

Chapters 9–14 shift to apocalyptic-messianic oracles. Zech 9:9 prophesies the king coming "lowly, and riding upon an ass" — quoted in Matt 21:5 and John 12:15 as fulfilled at the triumphal entry. Zech 11:12–13 prophesies the shepherd valued at thirty pieces of silver — fulfilled in Judas's betrayal (Matt 27:9–10). Zech 12:10 prophesies the pierced one "whom they have pierced" whom Jerusalem will mourn — cited in John 19:37 at the crucifixion. Zech 13:7 prophesies the struck shepherd and scattered sheep — cited by Jesus on the way to Gethsemane (Matt 26:31, Mark 14:27). Zech 14 closes with the LORD's feet on the Mount of Olives, the eschatological arrival of the king.

More NT messianic citations come from Zechariah than from any other Minor Prophet; in OT citation-density Zechariah is rivaled only by Isaiah and Psalms.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Zech 9:9 is the most widely-cited Zecharian prophecy — the triumphal-entry verse fulfilled literally in Matt 21 and John 12. Memorable, distinctive, short enough to render in full. Anchors the book's messianic weight while remaining accessible.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** Zechariah, son of Berechiah, son of Iddo (priestly lineage — Iddo returned with Zerubbabel per Neh 12:4, 16)
- **Date:** chs 1–8 dated 520–518 BC (three precise dates preserved); chs 9–14 undated, likely later in Zechariah's life or (per some scholarship) from a different author
- **Audience:** returned exiles in Jerusalem
- **Theme:** the LORD restores his people, returns to his temple, and his king will come lowly riding, pierced, struck, yet enthroned
- **Distinguishing notes:** densest messianic register of the Minor Prophets; two-part structure (chs 1–8 visionary / historical vs. chs 9–14 apocalyptic); eight night visions; two "burdens" (9:1, 12:1)

## 5. Chapter summary guidance

- Chs 1–6: eight night visions. Each chapter summary identifies the vision(s) it contains and the angel's interpretation. The "Branch" figure at 3:8 and 6:12 is messianic — flag without imposing full christology onto the original text.
- Ch 7–8: questions about fasting, the LORD's return to Zion. Transition to apocalyptic.
- Ch 9: **Zech 9:9 triumphal-entry prophecy** — flag prominently; Matt 21:5, John 12:15 fulfillment.
- Chs 10–11: shepherd / flock imagery; **Zech 11:12–13 thirty-pieces-of-silver prophecy** — Matt 27:9–10 fulfillment.
- Ch 12: **Zech 12:10 pierced-one prophecy** — John 19:37 fulfillment; Jerusalem's future mourning.
- Ch 13: **Zech 13:7 struck-shepherd prophecy** — Matt 26:31 fulfillment; the refined remnant.
- Ch 14: **LORD's Day**; Mount of Olives split; eschatological arrival of the king. Heavy §7.9 discipline — multiple interpretive schools on literal vs. spiritual fulfillment, millennial readings, etc.

## 9. Known contested points

- **Chs 9–14 authorship.** Traditional view: single Zechariah, later in his life. Critical proposal: "Deutero-Zechariah" (possibly multiple later authors) because of style shifts, different historical allusions (Greeks in 9:13, impossible for 520 BC), and absence of the visionary / angelic-interpreter structure. §7.9 discipline — present both; site does not adjudicate. Note that this question parallels the Isaiah authorship question being addressed in the retrofit (this batch's Section C).
- **Zech 9–14 eschatology.** Preterist, historicist, idealist, futurist readings of the apocalyptic material. Millennial readings of Zech 14. Mount of Olives split (14:4) — literal future geological event vs. symbolic. §7.9 discipline — Pastor Marc's drawer takes positions; hub does not.
- **"Pierced one" (12:10) antecedent.** "Me" (the LORD) piercing vs. the Messiah pierced vs. a prophetic figure. John 19:37's NT citation settles the Christian reading; the Hebrew text itself has textual-critical variants ("me" vs. "him"). Note without belaboring.
- **Zech 11:12–13 citation in Matt 27:9.** Matthew attributes the quotation to Jeremiah, not Zechariah. Traditional resolutions: Jeremiah as prophetic-head-of-scroll attribution; compound quotation mixing Jer 18–19 with Zech 11; textual tradition. Surface briefly.
