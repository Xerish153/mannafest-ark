## 1. Book identity

Habakkuk is the prophetic book as dialogue. The prophet asks God why violence and injustice persist in Judah (1:2–4); God answers that he is raising up the Babylonians to judge (1:5–11); Habakkuk asks why God would use a more wicked people to judge his own (1:12–2:1); God answers with a vision of the righteous living by faith while the proud falls (2:2–20); Habakkuk responds with a great prayer-psalm of trust (ch 3). The book is the theodicy dialogue of the prophetic corpus.

Hab 2:4 — "the just shall live by his faith" — is the NT's most-quoted OT verse on justification. Paul cites it in Rom 1:17 as the thesis of Romans, in Gal 3:11 as the core of his anti-legalism argument, and the writer of Hebrews cites it in Heb 10:38. Luther's rediscovery of justification-by-faith centered on this verse. The book's three chapters carry the proof-text of Reformation soteriology.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Hab 2:4 is theologically load-bearing far beyond the book's length — the NT's thrice-cited justification proof-text, the Pauline hermeneutical key, the Reformation anchor. Short, foundational, memorizable. The book's signature must be this verse.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** Habakkuk
- **Date:** c. 612–588 BC — after Assyria's fall (the Chaldeans/Babylonians are ascendant, 1:6) and before Jerusalem's fall
- **Audience:** Judah in the shadow of Babylonian threat
- **Theme:** the righteous live by faith when the world does not yet match the promise
- **Distinguishing notes:** dialogue structure (prophet/God/prophet/God); closes with psalm (ch 3 is inscribed with musical directions); Hab 2:4 as NT-foundational

## 5. Chapter summary guidance

- Ch 1: prophet's lament (vv 1–4) + God's reply about Babylonian judgment (vv 5–11) + prophet's counter-question (vv 12–17). Structure is the point.
- Ch 2: God's answer — the vision, "write it, make it plain," the just-shall-live-by-faith verse, five woes against the proud, "the earth shall be filled with the knowledge of the glory of the LORD." Summary flags Hab 2:4 and its NT usage.
- Ch 3: Habakkuk's prayer-psalm, theophany imagery, closing confession: "though the fig tree shall not blossom ... yet will I rejoice in the LORD." Musical superscriptions suggest liturgical use.

## 9. Known contested points

- **Hab 2:4 reading — "faith" or "faithfulness."** Hebrew `emunah` can be rendered either; LXX renders with `pistis` (faith/faithfulness, same Greek noun). Most Reformed readings take the human-directed-toward-God sense (faith); some readings emphasize the human faithfulness-to-God sense (faithfulness). Some readings (minority) read as God's faithfulness. Note the range; traditional Protestant reading: faith-in-God.
- **Ch 3's inclusion.** 1QpHab (the Qumran commentary) does not comment on ch 3 — leading some to propose ch 3 was a later liturgical addition. Majority view: original to Habakkuk; the Qumran commentary simply didn't treat the psalm material.
- **Date.** Within 612–588 BC range, exact dating debated. Note but don't pin.
