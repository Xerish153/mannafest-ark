## 1. Page identity

Paul names nine qualities in Gal 5:22–23 — love, joy, peace, longsuffering, gentleness, goodness, faith, meekness, temperance — and calls them *fruit* (singular, `karpos`), not *fruits* plural. The grammatical detail is load-bearing. In 1 Cor 12 Paul treats spiritual *gifts* as plural (`charismata`), variously distributed — one person has tongues, another has healing, another wisdom, no one has all. But in Gal 5 the fruit is singular: one fruit with nine facets, and the implication is that the Spirit grows all nine together in every believer, not one or two as specialty.

The contrast is structural. Gifts are differentiated; fruit is unified. Gifts are given; fruit is grown. Gifts emphasize what you can do; fruit emphasizes who you are becoming.

The page presents the singular fruit as a tree, with nine facets visible. The three-triad organization (inward / outward / Godward — the traditional Reformed reading, contested) gives structural shape. The contrast with Gal 5:19–21 "works of the flesh" shows the moral alternative. The John 15:1–8 vine imagery grounds the organic metaphor — fruit requires vine; abiding is not optional.

Tone: cumulative, devotional, structurally clear. A page a student could study one facet at a time.

## 3. Depth 2 framework sections

### Section 1 — The grammatical key: `karpos` singular

- Gal 5:22 Greek reads `ho de karpos tou pneumatos estin agapē, chara, eirēnē...` — *the fruit* (singular) *of the Spirit is love, joy, peace...*
- Not `karpoi` (plural). Deliberate grammatical choice.
- Contrast with 1 Cor 12:4 `diaireseis de charismatōn eisin` — *diversities of gifts* (plural) — and the successive "to one ... to another ..." of 12:8–10 (one person has wisdom, another has faith, another has healings). Gifts are distributed; fruit is not.
- The theological implication: the Spirit does not give love to one believer and peace to another as separate portfolios. The Spirit grows the whole fruit in every believer, over time, as the single organic process of sanctification.

### Section 2 — The nine facets surveyed with OT parallels

Each facet is a short entry at depth 2; fuller treatment in drilldowns.

- **Love** (`agapē`) — Heb *hesed* (covenant loyalty); Deut 6:5; Lev 19:18; 1 Cor 13.
- **Joy** (`chara`) — Heb *simchah*; Ps 16:11; Neh 8:10 ("the joy of the LORD is your strength"); Hab 3:17–18.
- **Peace** (`eirēnē`) — Heb *shalom*; Isa 9:6 (Prince of Peace); Ps 29:11; Num 6:26.
- **Longsuffering** (`makrothumia`) — Heb *erek appayim* ("slow to anger"); Ex 34:6; Num 14:18; Ps 86:15. God's own self-description.
- **Gentleness** (`chrēstotēs`) — Heb *tov* (goodness with kindness); Ps 34:8 ("O taste and see that the LORD is good"); Ps 145:9.
- **Goodness** (`agathōsunē`) — moral-quality goodness; Ps 23:6 ("goodness and mercy shall follow me"); Neh 9:25; Rom 15:14.
- **Faith** (`pistis`) — Heb *emunah* (faithfulness / firmness); Hab 2:4 ("the just shall live by his faith"); Lam 3:23 ("great is thy faithfulness"). Note: `pistis` here carries the sense of faithfulness (reliability) more than saving-trust.
- **Meekness** (`prautēs`) — Heb *anav*; Num 12:3 (Moses); Matt 5:5 (beatitude); Ps 25:9 ("the meek will he guide").
- **Temperance** (`egkrateia`) — self-control; Prov 16:32 ("he that ruleth his spirit than he that taketh a city"); Prov 25:28.

### Section 3 — Contrast with the works of the flesh (Gal 5:19–21)

- Paul's structural foil immediately precedes the fruit list. Verses 19–21 enumerate fifteen "works of the flesh" — adultery, fornication, uncleanness, lasciviousness, idolatry, witchcraft, hatred, variance, emulations, wrath, strife, seditions, heresies, envyings, murders, drunkenness, revellings.
- "Works" (`erga`, plural) of the flesh vs. "fruit" (`karpos`, singular) of the Spirit. The linguistic contrast amplifies the theological one.
- Works of the flesh are listed as discrete sinful products; fruit of the Spirit is one unified character.
- Moral-anthropology implication: apart from the Spirit, the flesh produces fragments (works); in the Spirit, the believer is grown whole (fruit).

### Section 4 — The organic metaphor: John 15:1–8 vine

- Fruit requires vine. Jesus in John 15:1–8 uses the fruit-bearing imagery explicitly. "I am the true vine, and my Father is the husbandman ... Abide in me, and I in you. As the branch cannot bear fruit of itself, except it abide in the vine; no more can ye, except ye abide in me."
- Theological implication: the fruit of the Spirit is not manufactured by effort; it is produced by abiding. The believer does not grow the fruit; the vine grows the fruit through the branch.
- The typological connection to the OT vine imagery (Israel as vine — Ps 80:8–16, Isa 5:1–7, Jer 2:21) — Israel failed to bear fruit; Christ as true vine succeeds.
- Forward-link to `/featured/trees/tree-types-encyclopedia` vine entry.

### Section 5 — The three-triad organization (contested)

- **Traditional Reformed / devotional organization:** Godward (Love / Joy / Peace) + Outward (Longsuffering / Gentleness / Goodness) + Inward (Faith / Meekness / Temperance). This is the organization used in the depth-1 visual.
- **Alternative organization:** Love as primary, followed by three triads that each elaborate an aspect of love (Joy/Peace/Longsuffering as love-at-rest, Gentleness/Goodness/Faith as love-in-action, Meekness/Temperance/? as love-disciplining-self). Proposed by various preachers and commentators; not universal.
- **Alternative organization:** no structural grouping — the list is unsequenced, and any grouping is interpretive overlay. This is the most rigorous grammatical reading — Paul did not flag triads.
- Surface the three-triad organization as the page's working structure; note the alternatives honestly.

4. **`longsuffering`** — Gal 5:22 + Ex 34:6 + Num 14:18 + Ps 86:15 + 2 Pet 3:9. God's own self-description applied to believers.

5. **`gentleness`** — Gal 5:22 + Ps 34:8 + Ps 145:9 + 2 Cor 10:1. Note: some English translations render `chrēstotēs` as "kindness"; "gentleness" is the KJV.

6. **`goodness`** — Gal 5:22 + Ps 23:6 + Rom 15:14. `Agathōsunē` as moral-goodness with action-toward-others.

7. **`faith`** — Gal 5:22 + Hab 2:4 + Lam 3:23 + Heb 11. **Key note:** `pistis` here is best rendered "faithfulness" (reliability, trustworthiness) rather than "saving faith." Surface the translation point.

9. **`temperance`** — Gal 5:23 + Prov 16:32 + Prov 25:28 + 1 Cor 9:25 (Paul on athletic self-control) + 2 Pet 1:6. Self-control as Spirit-empowered, not merely willpower.

10. **`singularity-of-fruit-vs-plurality-of-gifts`** — the page's grammatical-theological climax. Gal 5:22 vs. 1 Cor 12 grammatical comparison. Gifts distributed vs. fruit unified. Every believer bears the whole fruit; no believer has all the gifts. Editor's Notes primary location.

## 10. Known contested points

1. **Three-triad organization.** Traditional (Godward / Outward / Inward) is one organization; Love-primary-then-triads is another; unsequenced is a third. Surface the traditional as working structure; note alternatives honestly.

2. **`pistis` as faith or faithfulness.** At Gal 5:22 `pistis` is best rendered "faithfulness" (reliability) rather than "saving faith." The KJV retains "faith"; modern translations often render "faithfulness." Pastoral-theological care in the drilldown.

3. **`chrēstotēs` as gentleness or kindness.** KJV "gentleness"; most modern translations "kindness." The Greek has both valences.

4. **Fruit as automatic or cultivated.** Whether the believer's role is entirely passive (abide in the vine; fruit happens) or whether there's a discipline-of-Spirit-dependence that requires active receptivity. Reformed tradition emphasizes grace-monergism; Wesleyan tradition emphasizes cooperation-with-grace. Surface without adjudicating.

5. **The fruit list as exhaustive or representative.** Whether these nine are the complete list or a representative sample of the Spirit's single moral-work. Paul's list elsewhere (Eph 5:9, Phil 1:11) uses different selections; the nine-list is Gal 5-specific.

6. **Relation to beatitudes.** Matt 5 beatitudes partially overlap (meekness, peacemaking, purity). Some commentators see the fruit list as a Christian-ethics echo of the beatitudes. Loose connection; not page-load-bearing.
