## 1. Book identity

Hebrews is the NT's most sustained Old-Testament argument — an extended demonstration that Jesus is "better than" every institution Judaism knew: better than angels, better than Moses, better than the Levitical priesthood, better than the Aaronic sacrifice, better than the old covenant itself. The book is the NT's most comprehensive exposition of Christ's priesthood and of the Tabernacle / Temple system as shadow of heavenly reality.

Hebrews is also the book with the most uncertain authorship in the canon. Paul, Apollos, Barnabas, Luke, Priscilla, and Clement of Rome have all been proposed. The book doesn't name its author. Hebrews' Greek is the most polished in the NT and differs stylistically from Paul's undisputed letters, which is the principal reason Pauline authorship is contested.

Hebrews carries the NT's longest direct OT quotation (Jer 31:31–34 in Heb 8:8–12) and its most sustained Tabernacle exposition (Heb 8–10). It cross-anchors heavily into Wave F feature pages: `/featured/covenants`, `/featured/tabernacle`, `/featured/taw` (Heb 11:28 Passover), `/featured/armor-of-god` (Heb 4:12 word-as-sword). And it carries some of Scripture's most theologically difficult warning passages (Heb 6:4–6, 10:26–31) — passages that divide Calvinist and Arminian readings fundamentally.

Tone: textually grounded, OT-literate, Christologically focused. Owen's *Exposition of Hebrews* is the load-bearing PD commentary.

## 3. Signature verse

**Primary:** Heb 4:14–16 — "Seeing then that we have a great high priest, that is passed into the heavens, Jesus the Son of God, let us hold fast our profession..."

This passage bridges the book's two halves (the "better than" comparisons of 1–7 and the sustained priesthood/tabernacle/sacrifice argument of 8–10) and captures the book's pastoral purpose — having-a-great-high-priest is the ground of perseverance.

**Alternative:** Heb 12:1–2 (the faith chapter's concluding exhortation). Also excellent.

Default to Heb 4:14–16 for the priesthood-Christology weight.

## 4. Stat strip

- **Author:** unknown. Paul (traditional — accepted by many through church history), Apollos (Luther's proposal), Barnabas (Tertullian's attribution), Priscilla (Harnack's 20th-c. proposal), Clement of Rome, Luke — all have defenders. **Site does not adjudicate.**
- **Approximate date:** mid-to-late 1st century, almost certainly before AD 70 (the book argues against the ongoing Temple liturgy as if the Temple still stands).
- **Audience:** Jewish Christians (as the title long suggests) facing pressure to abandon the gospel and return to Judaism. Alternative: mixed Jewish-Gentile community with Jewish-rooted concerns. Site default: Jewish-Christian primary audience.
- **Theme (one line):** "Jesus is better. Hold fast."
- **Chapter count:** 13.
- **Distinguishing notes:** NT's most sustained OT exegesis; polished Greek (possibly the NT's best); structural parallelism between expository sections and exhortation sections.

## 5. Depth 2 framework sections

1. **Better than angels and Moses (Heb 1–4)** — the OT catena of Heb 1, the "rest" argument climaxing in Heb 4.
2. **Better priesthood (Heb 4:14–7:28)** — Christ as high priest after the order of Melchizedek, not Aaron. The Melchizedek figure (Gen 14:18–20, Ps 110:4) unpacked.
3. **Better covenant (Heb 8)** — the Jer 31:31–34 quotation as the book's structural center. Cross-link to `/featured/covenants/new`.
4. **Better sanctuary and sacrifice (Heb 9–10)** — the earthly Tabernacle as shadow of the heavenly; Christ's once-for-all self-offering. Cross-link to `/featured/tabernacle`.
5. **Faith and endurance (Heb 11–12)** — the faith chapter surveying OT exemplars, the "great cloud of witnesses," the discipline-endurance appeal.
6. **Pastoral close (Heb 13)** — practical exhortations; hospitality; marriage; leaders; closing benediction.

**The warning passages — cross-cutting** (Heb 2:1–4, 3:7–19, 6:4–6, 10:26–31, 12:25–29) — surfaced as a cross-cutting theme in framework section 5 or 6, with §7.9 discipline: Calvinist reading (the passages warn genuine believers against apostasy but the genuinely regenerate cannot ultimately fall away — perseverance of the saints), Arminian reading (the passages warn that genuine believers can lose salvation), Hypothetical reading (Owen's position — warnings describe a hypothetical the saints won't actually experience). All three presented; Pastor Marc's drawer may take a position.

## 8. Commentary tradition priority

- **Puritan — John Owen's *Exposition of Hebrews*** (PD, 7 volumes, written 1668–1684). **The load-bearing commentary for this book.** Owen's Hebrews is considered by many the greatest English-language commentary on any biblical book. Feature Owen on most chapters; Show other voices reveals the rest.

## 10. Known contested points (surface honestly)

1. **Authorship.** Paul / Apollos / Barnabas / Priscilla / Luke / Clement — all proposed. Present the top 3 or 4 candidates with their strongest historical advocates; do not endorse a single attribution. Site default: "traditional attribution Pauline, modern scholarship varies — the book itself does not name its author."
2. **Warning passages (Heb 6:4–6, 10:26–31).** Three major readings: Calvinist (genuine believers warned but will persevere), Arminian (genuine believers can apostatize), Hypothetical-Owenian (warning describes a hypothetical the elect won't experience). Present all three; §7.9 discipline; Pastor Marc's drawer may take a position.
3. **Sabbath-rest interpretation (Heb 4).** Literal Sabbath / present-rest-in-Christ / eschatological-rest. Usually held as layered meanings rather than either/or; present nuance.
4. **Melchizedek identity (Heb 7).** Historical figure (Gen 14) / Christophany (some Patristic readings) / purely typological figure. Majority view: historical person functioning as type of Christ; surface the other views without endorsement.
5. **Jewish-Christian audience vs. mixed.** Primary audience debate — traditional view specifies Jewish-Christian, some recent proposals argue mixed. Site default: Jewish-Christian primary.
6. **Heb 9:27 "once to die" and purgatory / intermediate state.** Catholic and some Anglo-Catholic readings surface the passage in purgatory discussion; Reformed readings reject purgatory altogether. Note if it arises in chapter summary; don't fuel the debate beyond what the text requires.
