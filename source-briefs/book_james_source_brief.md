## 1. Book identity

James is the New Testament's wisdom literature — a short, practical letter in the genre of Proverbs more than of Pauline argument. The traditional author is James, brother of Jesus and leader of the Jerusalem church (the James of Acts 15, the James Paul names in Gal 1:19 and 2:9). The letter is likely very early (c. 45–50 AD), possibly the earliest NT document, addressed to "the twelve tribes which are scattered abroad" — Jewish Christians outside Judea.

The letter's structure is loose, proverb-like, and aggressively practical. Themes circulate: enduring trials, hearing vs. doing, tongue-control, rich-and-poor, wisdom from above, prayer, patience, the power of the tongue again, the sin of partiality. James 1:22 — "be ye doers of the word, and not hearers only, deceiving your own selves" — captures the letter's insistence that Christianity without observable conduct is self-deception.

The book's most famous section is James 2:14–26 on faith-and-works: "what doth it profit, my brethren, though a man say he hath faith, and have not works? can faith save him? ... faith without works is dead." Luther's 1522 preface to James famously called it "an epistle of straw" because James seemed to contradict Paul's justification-by-faith-alone. The classical Protestant resolution: Paul speaks of how one is justified initially before God (by faith alone, in Christ's righteousness imputed); James speaks of how genuine faith shows itself to be genuine (by its fruit). They are not addressing the same question.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

James 1:22 is the letter's thesis statement — hearing-without-doing as self-deception. Short, pointed, canonical. Captures James's posture without the Pauline-contrast complications of 2:14–26.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** James, brother of Jesus (traditional; James the Just of Jerusalem); alternative early attributions include James son of Zebedee (rejected by most since his martyrdom c. 44 AD precedes the letter's circulation)
- **Date:** c. 45–50 AD (one of the earliest NT documents; some date slightly later)
- **Audience:** "the twelve tribes which are scattered abroad" (1:1) — diaspora Jewish Christians
- **Theme:** practical wisdom; the tongue, wealth, prayer, trials; faith that works
- **Distinguishing notes:** wisdom-literature genre; loose thematic structure; the faith-and-works passage (2:14–26) famous for Luther's critical comments; most Jewish of NT letters

## 9. Known contested points

- **Authorship identity.** "James" in 1:1 could be: James son of Zebedee (rejected for chronological reasons), James son of Alphaeus, James brother of Jesus, or an anonymous "James." Traditional and majority-conservative: James brother of Jesus. Surface briefly.
- **Faith-and-works reconciliation with Paul.** Classical Reformed: James speaks of the fruit, Paul of the root. Roman Catholic reading often takes James as requiring works as contributing to justification. Note the confessional divide; site presents the Protestant reading as primary without polemic.
- **Luther's "epistle of straw."** Luther's critical assessment was part of his 1522 NT preface; he later softened but never fully retracted. Surface honestly; don't defend or disparage the comment as a whole historical moment.
- **Date of composition.** Pre-Jerusalem Council (c. 49 AD, Acts 15) or post-Council. Pre-Council is the majority conservative position given silence on Gentile-inclusion questions that dominate post-Council letters. Surface.
