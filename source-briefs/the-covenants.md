## Page intent

Covenant is the spine of the Bible. The word appears 300+ times; every major movement of redemptive history is structured around the cutting, keeping, breaking, and renewing of covenants. But modern readers hear "covenant" as a synonym for "contract" or "agreement," and the Hebrew reality dissolves.

The page recovers the biblical category. A *berith* — the Hebrew word translated covenant — is not a contract. It is a blood-bond between parties, enacted by ritual sacrifice, binding the parties unto death. Hebrew idiom does not "make" a covenant; it *cuts* one — *karath berith* — because the ritual requires animals to be cut in half, the parties passing between the pieces, swearing: *let it be done to me as it was done to these if I break this bond*.

Once that category is in place, the six covenants of scripture become legible as the progressive architecture of God's self-binding to His people. Adamic, Noahic, Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, New — each one builds on the last, each one deepens God's commitment, and together they culminate in the New Covenant sealed by Christ's blood.

The aesthetic thesis: a vertical timeline of the six covenants descending through redemptive history, rendered with the scarlet thread of blood connecting them. A covenant is cut by blood; the thread that binds them all is the blood that seals each one and ultimately Christ's own.

The page's editorial posture: covenants are God's self-binding. God is not obligated to commit Himself to His people. He chooses to — and chooses the most severe form of commitment available in the ancient world, one sealed in blood and binding unto death. The doctrine of covenant is the doctrine of God's chosen vulnerability on behalf of His people.

Distinct from Names of God (who God is) and Tabernacle (where God dwells): this page is about **the formal bonds by which God attaches Himself to His people** across redemptive history.

## Depth 2 — Framework

Six framework sections.

### 2.1 Karath berith — to cut a covenant

Featured spotlight section. The page's central grammatical and conceptual correction.

**The Hebrew idiom:**

Hebrew does not "make" a covenant. Hebrew *cuts* a covenant — *karath berith* (כָּרַת בְּרִית). The verb *karath* means "to cut" or "to slaughter." The construction is used more than 80 times in the Old Testament. The translation "make a covenant" obscures the violence at the ceremony's heart.

**The ritual (Gen 15 as primary example):**

When Abram asks for assurance of God's promise (Gen 15:8), God instructs him to bring a heifer, a goat, a ram, a turtledove, and a young pigeon. Abram cuts the animals in half (Gen 15:10), arranges the pieces in two parallel rows, and waits. At sunset, a deep sleep falls on Abram. Then a *"smoking furnace, and a burning lamp"* — manifestations of God's presence — *pass between the pieces* (Gen 15:17).

This is the covenant ritual. Two parties walk between the halves of slaughtered animals, invoking the implicit oath: *let it be done to me as it was done to these if I break this bond.* Jeremiah 34:18 names the ritual explicitly, condemning those who *"cut the calf in twain, and passed between the parts thereof."*

**Why the ritual matters:**

A covenant is not a contract. A contract is breakable — parties can withdraw with penalty clauses. A covenant is a blood-bond *unto death*. The parties have walked through the pieces. There is no "dissolving" a covenant without violating the oath, and the oath's penalty is death.

**The unique theology of Genesis 15:**

In the Genesis 15 ritual, Abram never walks between the pieces. He falls into a deep sleep. Only God — manifested as the smoking furnace and burning lamp — passes through. This is the astonishing fact: God cuts a unilateral covenant with Abraham, binding *Himself* to the promise while Abraham is unconscious. The covenant's weight rests entirely on God. If the covenant fails, only God bears the curse.

This is the covenant paradigm that culminates at Calvary: God binding Himself unilaterally to His people, bearing the covenant curse Himself when His people fail to keep it.

**Why this matters for how the whole Bible reads:**

Every time a covenant is established in scripture, blood is shed. Not as symbol but as substance. The covenant *is* the blood. Hebrews 9:22 — *"without shedding of blood is no remission"* — is not a narrow statement about sin. It is the fundamental logic of covenant: covenants are sealed by death, and the New Covenant Christ sealed in His own blood is the final, definitive *karath berith*.

Featured commentary voice: **John Calvin**, *Commentary on Genesis* and *Institutes* III.21 — the Reformed treatment of covenant as the structuring category of redemptive history. Backup: **Herman Witsius**, *The Economy of the Covenants* (1677, fully PD).

### 2.2 The four elements of every covenant

Featured framework section. Once the ritual category is in place, the shape of every biblical covenant becomes legible. Every covenant contains four elements:

1. **Parties** — who is binding themselves to whom
2. **Terms** — what each party commits to
3. **Blood** — the sacrifice that seals the bond
4. **Sign** — the visible marker that memorializes the covenant

Card cluster — four cards, one per element. Each card displays the element, its function, and examples across the six covenants.

**Parties:**
- Adamic — God and humanity
- Noahic — God, Noah's family, and all creation
- Abrahamic — God and Abraham's seed
- Mosaic — God and Israel as a nation
- Davidic — God and the Davidic dynasty
- New — God and all who are in Christ

**Terms:**
- Vary by covenant, but always involve God's promise and (sometimes) the people's response
- Some covenants are *unilateral* — God binds Himself without requiring reciprocal obligation (Noahic, Abrahamic, Davidic, New)
- Some are *bilateral* — conditional blessing/curse based on response (Mosaic)

**Blood:**
- Every covenant is sealed by sacrifice
- Genesis 3:21 — the first sacrifice (animal skins for Adam and Eve, implying the death of animals) immediately follows the Fall
- Genesis 8:20 — Noah sacrifices clean animals immediately after leaving the ark
- Genesis 15 — animals cut in pieces for the Abrahamic ritual
- Exodus 24:8 — Moses sprinkles blood on the people: *"This is the blood of the covenant"*
- 1 Samuel / 2 Samuel — Davidic sacrifices at the establishment of the throne
- Luke 22:20 — Christ: *"This cup is the new testament in my blood"*

**Sign:**
- Every covenant has a visible marker that memorializes it
- Noahic — the rainbow (Gen 9:13)
- Abrahamic — circumcision (Gen 17:10–11)
- Mosaic — the Sabbath (Exod 31:13, 16–17)
- Davidic — the Davidic throne itself
- New — the Lord's Table (Luke 22:19–20; 1 Cor 11:23–26)

**Why this structure matters:**

Once you can see the four elements in every covenant, the page's central claim becomes undeniable: the covenants are a unified architecture, not a random series. Each new covenant preserves the prior ones' elements while intensifying the bond. Blood is always shed. Signs are always given. Terms progress toward the New Covenant's interior transformation.

### 2.3 Unilateral vs bilateral — grace and law

Brief framework section.

Of the six major covenants, five are fundamentally unilateral (God binds Himself to keep His word regardless of human response) and one is fundamentally bilateral (conditional blessing/curse based on Israel's obedience).

- **Unilateral:** Adamic, Noahic, Abrahamic, Davidic, New
- **Bilateral:** Mosaic

This is theologically significant. The unilateral covenants rest entirely on God's faithfulness. The Mosaic covenant — the one Israel broke, generating centuries of prophetic lament — is the exception, not the rule. When Paul argues in Galatians 3:15–18 that the Mosaic Law (given 430 years after Abraham) cannot cancel the Abrahamic promise, this is why: the Abrahamic covenant was unilateral and irrevocable; the Mosaic was conditional and time-bound.

The New Covenant, like the Abrahamic it ultimately fulfills, is unilateral. God writes His law on the heart (Jer 31:33); God remembers sin no more (Jer 31:34); God seals the bond in His own Son's blood. The people's response is faith, not the ground of the covenant but its entry point.

### 2.4 "Without shedding of blood there is no remission"

Featured framework section. Hebrews 9:22 as the page's central theological summary.

**The text:**

> *"And almost all things are by the law purged with blood; and without shedding of blood is no remission."* — Hebrews 9:22

**What the verse says:**

The author of Hebrews is summarizing Levitical ritual and projecting forward to Christ. Under the Mosaic covenant, virtually everything — the tabernacle, its furniture, the priesthood, the people — was cleansed and consecrated by blood. The Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16; cf. **Scarlet Thread** §2.2) was the annual reset of the entire covenant system through blood applied to the mercy seat.

But Hebrews makes the deeper claim: this was not arbitrary ritual. Blood is the substance that seals covenants and remits sin because *covenants are blood-bonds* and *sin violates the covenant*. Without blood there is no remission because without blood there is no covenant, and outside the covenant there is no forgiveness.

**Christ's blood as the final covenant-cutting:**

Hebrews 9–10 argues that Christ's blood accomplishes what animal blood could not. The animal sacrifices reiterated the covenant annually; Christ's sacrifice establishes the New Covenant *once for all* (Heb 10:10). The blood Christ shed at the cross is simultaneously:
- The covenant-cutting blood that establishes the New Covenant (Heb 9:15–17)
- The atoning blood that remits sin (Heb 9:22)
- The blood that speaks better things than Abel's (Heb 12:24) — cross-link to **Scarlet Thread** §2.1

**Why this lands the page:**

The whole covenant architecture of scripture points to this: a final, definitive covenant-cutting in which God Himself takes the curse of covenant violation, bearing in His own Son's body what should have fallen on His covenant-breaking people. The Genesis 15 pattern — God walking through the pieces while Abraham slept — finds its fulfillment at the cross, where God alone bears the weight of a covenant no human could keep.

Featured commentary voice: **John Owen**, *Exposition of Hebrews* (4 volumes, fully PD; the most thorough PD treatment of the covenant theology of Hebrews).

### 2.5 The New Covenant — Jeremiah 31 and its fulfillment

Featured framework section.

**Jeremiah's prophecy (Jer 31:31–34):**

Six hundred years before Christ, Jeremiah prophesies a New Covenant. Four distinctive features:

1. **Not like the Mosaic covenant** — *"which my covenant they brake"* (v.32); the New Covenant will not be bilateral in the Mosaic sense
2. **Law written on the heart** — *"I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts"* (v.33); interior transformation, not external compulsion
3. **Direct knowledge of God** — *"they shall all know me, from the least of them unto the greatest"* (v.34); every member of the covenant has personal relationship with God
4. **Total forgiveness** — *"I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more"* (v.34); sin not just forgiven but forgotten

**Christ's claim at the Last Supper (Luke 22:20):**

> *"This cup is the new testament in my blood, which is shed for you."*

The Greek word translated "testament" (*diathēkē*) is the same word the Septuagint uses for the Hebrew *berith* (covenant). Christ is explicitly claiming to inaugurate Jeremiah's prophesied New Covenant — in His own blood.

**The apostolic declaration (Hebrews 8–10):**

The book of Hebrews quotes Jeremiah 31 in full (Heb 8:8–12) and builds an entire argument around it: Christ is the mediator of a better covenant, established on better promises, sealed by a better sacrifice. The old covenant is *"ready to vanish away"* (Heb 8:13); the new is eternal.

**What the New Covenant produces:**

- **Forgiveness** — sin remembered no more (cross-link to any future page on the Day of Atonement / Yom Kippur)
- **The indwelling Spirit** — the "law written on the heart" is fulfilled in the gift of the Holy Spirit (cross-link to **Fruit of the Spirit**)
- **Direct access to God** — the torn veil (cross-link to **Tabernacle** §2.3)
- **Eternal inheritance** — the irrevocable bond culminating in the New Jerusalem (cross-link to any future page on Creation to New Creation)

### 2.6 "In covenant" — the pastoral implication

Brief framework section.

When scripture speaks of believers being "in Christ" (a phrase Paul uses 164+ times in his letters), the underlying theology is covenant. To be "in Christ" is to be in covenant with God through Christ — to be bound into the covenant He mediated, sealed in His blood, and secured by His resurrection.

This has immediate pastoral consequences:

- **The covenant is God's commitment, not yours.** You did not cut the covenant; Christ did. You entered it by faith, but the covenant's weight rests on God's oath, not on your performance.
- **Your failures do not break it.** Mosaic violations brought curses because the Mosaic covenant was bilateral. The New Covenant is unilateral. Your failures grieve the Spirit and cost you fellowship, but they do not nullify the bond. Romans 8:1: *"there is therefore now no condemnation to them which are in Christ Jesus."*
- **You are kept by covenant, not by willpower.** The covenant holds you; you do not hold the covenant. The Spirit who sealed you (Eph 1:13) keeps you until the day of redemption (Eph 4:30).
- **Assurance is covenant-grounded.** You can know you are saved because you are in a covenant God has bound Himself to keep — not because your experience is strong, your feelings are right, or your performance is adequate.

This is the pastoral freedom of the covenant category. The blood-bond God has cut in Christ is more secure than any human commitment could ever be — because God Himself bears its whole weight.
