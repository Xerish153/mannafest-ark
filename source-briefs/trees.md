## 1. Page identity

Humanity's story is told in three trees. Two stood in Eden and framed the first choice. One stood on Calvary and absorbed the consequence of the wrong one. A fourth tree — the tree of life again, now multiplied and freely accessible — closes the canon in Revelation 22. The arc is the arc of the Bible: access granted, access forfeited, access restored.

The page's job is to make the arc visible in a single glance, then walk the student through the typological logic that binds the three trees into one argument, then open the evidence one verse at a time. The ELS introduction subsection is included here because the claim "the Torah's word for 'tree' contains equidistant-letter patterns spelling other key words" falls naturally under a page that is already about Scripture's engagement with tree-imagery.

Tone: understatement. The arc speaks for itself if the visual is right.

## 3. Depth 2 framework sections

### Section 1 — Gen 2–3: two trees, two choices, the expulsion

- Gen 2:9 — both trees in the midst of the garden, adjacent access
- Gen 2:16–17 — free access to every tree including tree of life; only tree of knowledge forbidden
- Gen 3:1–6 — the serpent's question, the choice, the eating
- Gen 3:22–24 — God's stated reason for expulsion: "lest he put forth his hand, and take also of the tree of life, and eat, and live for ever." Cherubim and flaming sword bar access from that moment.
- **Surface honestly:** the text implies Adam and Eve had not yet eaten from the tree of life before the fall (3:22's "lest he put forth his hand" — future contingency, not retrospective). Jewish tradition disagrees at points; Rashi (PD) reads 3:22 as partly referring to Adam's attempt to reach for the tree after the fall.
- Reformed (Calvin on Gen 3), Puritan (Henry on Gen 3)

### Section 2 — The cross as tree language

- **Deut 21:23** — "he that is hanged is accursed of God." The underlying legal principle.
- **Gal 3:13** — "Christ hath redeemed us from the curse of the law, being made a curse for us: for it is written, Cursed is every one that hangeth on a tree." Paul cites Deut 21:23 and names the cross as tree.
- **1 Pet 2:24** — "bare our sins in his body on the tree."
- **Acts 5:30, 10:39, 13:29** — Luke's recorded apostolic preaching consistently uses `xylon` (tree / wood) for the cross. This is not incidental diction; it is typological claim.
- The OT-NT inversion: what was curse becomes cure. The tree that kills (Gen 2:17) finds its structural inverse in the tree that saves (Gal 3:13).

### Section 3 — Rev 2:7 and 22:2: tree of life restored and multiplied

- **Rev 2:7** — "To him that overcometh will I give to eat of the tree of life, which is in the midst of the paradise of God." Access restored to the overcomer.
- **Rev 22:2** — "In the midst of the street of it, and on either side of the river, was there the tree of life, which bare twelve manner of fruits, and yielded her fruit every month: and the leaves of the tree were for the healing of the nations."
- Eschatological resolution of Gen 3:22–24: the sword no longer turns, access is no longer barred, the tree is no longer singular. Multiplication ("on either side") and constancy ("every month") are visual intensifications of what was lost.
- Rev 22:14 — "that they may have right to the tree of life." The restored access is made explicit.
- Commentary: Seiss (PD), Bullinger (PD), Spurgeon on Rev where extant, Henry on Rev.

### Section 4 — Tree-types encyclopedia

A typology index — other trees named in Scripture that carry theological weight. Not exhaustive; representative.

- **Fig tree** — Gen 3:7 (Adam and Eve's coverings), Matt 21:18–22 (cursing the barren fig), John 1:48 (Nathanael under the fig), Mic 4:4 (peace imagery).
- **Olive tree** — Ps 52:8 (the righteous flourishing), Rom 11:16–24 (Israel and the Gentiles grafted), the menorah oil, Jer 11:16.
- **Cedar** — Ps 92:12 (the righteous like cedars), 1 Kings 5–7 (Solomon's temple construction), Ezek 17 (the cedar sprig allegory).
- **Vine as tree-imagery** — Ps 80:8–16 (Israel as vine), John 15:1–8 (Jesus as true vine). Vine is shrub-scale botanically but Scripture treats it with tree-weight.
- **Almond** — Num 17 (Aaron's rod budding), Ex 25:33–34 (menorah's almond blossoms), Jer 1:11–12 (the Lord "hastening" — wordplay on `shaqed` almond / `shoqed` watching).

Each a short entry (≤60 words) on the main page; fuller treatment in the `tree-types-encyclopedia` drilldown.

### Section 5 — ELS introduction subsection (per Vision §7.10)

**Required visual elements per §7.10:** at least three ELS (Equidistant Letter Sequence) findings rendered as interactive or graphical components, each a matrix with ELS-highlighted characters visually marked. Short 1-line source caption per pattern.

**Three patterns to render (pinned 2026-04-23):**

1. **Torah YHWH skip-49 pattern** — in the book of Genesis, beginning from certain tav letters, counting every 50th letter (skip-49) spells יהוה (YHWH). Source: Witztum, Rips, Rosenberg, "Equidistant Letter Sequences in the Book of Genesis," *Statistical Science* 9(3): 429–438 (1994). Academic citation. Short caption: "Skip-49 YHWH pattern, Genesis opening — Witztum-Rips-Rosenberg 1994."
2. **Genesis 1:1 prime-number numerical structures** — Ivan Panin's analysis of the seven Hebrew words / 28 letters of Gen 1:1 revealing extensive divisibility by 7 and prime-number patterns. Source: Ivan Panin, *Bible Numerics* (early 20th c., PD). Short caption: "Gen 1:1 prime-pattern structure — Panin numerical analysis, PD."
3. **"Torah" skip-50 pattern in Exodus / Numbers / Deuteronomy** — starting from the first ת in each book, counting every 50th letter spells תורה (Torah) in Genesis, Exodus, Numbers, Deuteronomy. (Leviticus is the exception; the claim there is that YHWH appears at short intervals — a pattern noted by Panin and later ELS researchers.) Source: Panin + later ELS replications in Witztum tradition. Short caption: "Torah skip-50 pattern, Pentateuchal bookends — Panin + ELS replication tradition."

**Deliberately NOT surfaced at this level:** Isaiah 53 ELS "Yeshua" findings (Rambsel 1996 and successors). Those claims are under copyright (Rambsel d. 2007) and not PD-safe for reproduction. If a student wants the Isaiah 53 ELS material, link them to the published books as pointer-only citations ≤15 words; do not render the patterns on-site.

**No paragraph-length scholarly defense at depth 2.** Two sentence cards — one for the pattern claim, one for the counter-claim (McKay et al.'s randomization critique). Full debate deferred to `els-introduction` drilldown.

## 10. Known contested points (surface honestly)

1. **Tree of life access pre-fall.** Whether Adam and Eve ate from the tree of life before the fall is genuinely uncertain from the Hebrew text. Gen 3:22's "lest he put forth his hand" most naturally reads as future-contingent (they hadn't yet eaten), but Jewish tradition (Rashi at points) reads differently. Surface honestly; do not pretend the question is settled.

2. **"Did they die that day?"** (Gen 2:17 vs. Gen 5:5 — Adam lived 930 years.) Classical answers include: immediate spiritual death with delayed physical death (Augustine's standard reading); the Hebrew `moth tamuth` as "dying you shall die" (an inceptive / emphatic aspect); covenantal death separating them from God. Present all three as real readings; do not adjudicate.

3. **ELS findings generally.** Genuinely contested in the academic literature. Witztum-Rips-Rosenberg 1994 passed peer review at *Statistical Science* but the 1999 McKay et al. critique ran in the same journal and raised serious methodological concerns. The honest posture is: interesting enough to note, uncertain enough not to rest doctrinal weight on. Founder editorial note lives here.

4. **Tree-types encyclopedia scope.** This drilldown could expand indefinitely. Scope it to the five typologies listed; parking-lot additions for later (sycamore / Zacchaeus, gopher wood / ark, pine / oak / terebinth) without expanding the initial ship.

5. **Cross as `xylon` vs. `stauros`.** The Greek NT uses both terms for the cross. `Xylon` (tree / wood) is Luke's consistent preaching vocabulary; `stauros` (cross / stake) is the more common noun. The typological argument rests on the `xylon` usage. Note this textual detail without overstating the etymological argument.
