## 1. Book identity

Ezekiel is the exile prophet — priest-turned-prophet, carried to Babylon in the 597 BC deportation (the second wave, before Jerusalem's final fall in 586), writing to a community that still thinks the exile is temporary and judgment is for someone else. His visions are among Scripture's strangest: the wheels within wheels and the four living creatures (Ezek 1), the glory of God departing the Temple in stages (Ezek 8–11), the valley of dry bones coming to life (Ezek 37), the detailed millennial-or-symbolic Temple (Ezek 40–48). His symbolic actions are among its most embodied: lying on his side for 430 days (Ezek 4), shaving his head with a sword and burning the hair (Ezek 5), eating his food in anxiety (Ezek 12). He survives his wife's death without mourning as a sign to the people (Ezek 24).

The book carries one of the OT's most load-bearing new-covenant texts — Ezek 36:26–27, "a new heart and a new spirit" — and announces the restoration of Israel through the dry-bones vision of Ezek 37. It ends with the name that closes the book and gives the restored city its identity: YHWH Shammah, "the LORD is there" (Ezek 48:35).

Tone: sober, textually anchored, interpretively cautious. Ezekiel is highly contested in its latter chapters (Gog and Magog, the millennial Temple), and the hub needs to present interpretive options without taking sides.

## 3. Signature verse

**Primary:** Ezek 36:26–27 — "A new heart also will I give you, and a new spirit will I put within you..."

This passage is theologically twinned with Jer 31:31–34 and together they form the OT's most explicit new-covenant anticipation. Heb 8 quotes Jeremiah; Paul's pneumatology (Rom 8, 2 Cor 3) draws from Ezekiel. Surface the full two-verse passage on the signature panel.

**Alternative (if Pastor Marc prefers something shorter):** Ezek 37:27 or Ezek 48:35. Default to 36:26–27.

## 4. Stat strip

- **Author:** Ezekiel, son of Buzi, priest (Ezek 1:3).
- **Approximate date of composition:** 593–571 BC (Ezek 1:1 fifth year of Jehoiachin's exile = 593 BC; last dated oracle Ezek 29:17 = 571 BC).
- **Audience:** Judean exiles in Babylon, especially along the Chebar canal (Ezek 1:1).
- **Theme (one line):** "The glory departs, the glory returns, and the LORD is there."
- **Chapter count:** 48.
- **Distinguishing notes:** chronologically ordered (largely) — unlike Jeremiah; heavy use of apocalyptic-visionary mode; concludes with the detailed Temple vision (40–48) that stands apart from the rest of the book in content and tone.

## 5. Depth 2 framework sections

1. **Call and wheels-vision (Ezek 1–3)** — the inaugural theophany, the four living creatures, the commissioning as watchman. Set in its historical context (fifth year of Jehoiachin's captivity).
2. **The glory departs from the Temple (Ezek 8–11)** — the staged withdrawal of God's presence. Set against the backdrop of Jerusalem's continued abominations. The theological inverse of Ezek 43's glory returning to the new Temple.
3. **Judgment oracles against Israel and the nations (Ezek 4–32)** — the symbolic actions (4–5, 12, 24), the oracles against Jerusalem (8–24), the oracles against the nations (25–32). Not a chapter-by-chapter walk; a framework orientation for readers entering the judgment section.
4. **The Valley of Dry Bones and the restoration of Israel (Ezek 37)** — the vision of resurrection, the joining of the two sticks (Judah and Israel), the Davidic prince, the eternal covenant. Cross-link to `/featured/covenants`.
5. **Gog and Magog (Ezek 38–39)** — surfaced per §7.9 as contested. Competing identifications (historical Gog vs end-times adversary vs symbolic forces of chaos) are all surfaced with source-citation; site does not adjudicate.
6. **The Temple Vision (Ezek 40–48)** — the detailed architectural and liturgical vision. Per §7.9: literal millennial Temple reading (dispensational — Seiss surfaces this on similar material in Rev), symbolic-of-the-Church reading (Reformed — Calvin), typological-but-not-literal reading (amill). All three frameworks named at depth 2; Pastor Marc's drawer may take a position.

- **Note dated oracles** — many chapters begin with a specific year/month/day reference; preserve the dating.

## 10. Known contested points (surface honestly)

1. **Gog and Magog identification.** Competing readings: (a) a specific ancient-world enemy (Gog of Lydia, per some historical identifications); (b) a future end-times adversary (premillennial reading, post-millennial battle before the thousand-year reign, or pre-millennial battle during the tribulation); (c) symbolic forces of chaos gathered against God's people (amillennial reading). Site surfaces all three; no adjudication.
2. **Millennial Temple (Ezek 40–48).** (a) Literal future Temple during a millennial reign (dispensational); (b) figurative / symbolic representation of the eschatological worship of God (amillennial); (c) typological anticipation of the Church as God's dwelling (Reformed covenant-theology). §7.9 framework — present each with its strongest defenders; Pastor Marc's drawer may take a position.
3. **Animal sacrifices in Ezek 40–46.** If the millennial Temple is literal, why sacrifices in a post-Calvary economy? Dispensational responses (memorial-commemorative sacrifices) are surfaced; Reformed responses (non-literal reading resolves the problem) are surfaced; tension honestly named.
4. **Ezek 28:11–19 "King of Tyre" as possible Satan-reference.** The "thou wast in Eden the garden of God" language has provoked the reading that this passage describes Satan's pre-fall state. Competing view: it's extended royal-hyperbole against a human king of Tyre, using Eden imagery for rhetorical effect. Surface both; no adjudication.
5. **Ezek 13:17–23 "women who prophesy" and Ezek 4:4–8 "430 days" scope.** Minor interpretive points; note where relevant in chapter summaries.
