## 1. Book identity

3 John is the NT's shortest book by word count — a single-chapter personal letter of fourteen verses from "the elder" (traditional: John the apostle) to a Christian named Gaius. Date: c. 85–95 AD, from Ephesus in traditional reading, companion to 2 John.

The letter is a window into early church authority dynamics. Three figures orbit the short text: Gaius (commended for hospitality to traveling teachers, 1:5–8), Diotrephes (condemned for refusing hospitality and excommunicating those who receive the traveling teachers, 1:9–11), and Demetrius (commended by all, 1:12). Diotrephes's refusal to receive John's envoys and his active ejection of hospitable believers from the church gives 3 John its distinctive edge: a local authority figure opposing apostolic oversight, John promising to confront him directly when he comes (1:10).

The letter's theological core is 3 John 1:4 — "I have no greater joy than to hear that my children walk in truth" — the paternal pastoral affection that carries the whole brief text. The practical theme is that authentic Christian community supports itinerant ministry rather than obstructs it, and that church authority figures can themselves be in need of correction.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

3 John 1:4 captures the letter's paternal affection for believers walking in truth. Short, memorable, self-contained. Widely quoted in pastoral contexts.

- Diotrephes historical identification: PD sources only; don't speculate beyond text

## 9. Known contested points

- **Diotrephes's status.** Local church leader / bishop-like figure / wealthy patron. Text doesn't specify. Most PD commentary: a local church's dominant figure whose authority John challenges. Note without pinning.
- **"The elder" identity.** Same question as 2 John — John the apostle (traditional) or John the elder as separate figure (Papias / Eusebius). Traditional majority position.
- **3 John 1:2 "prosperity" reading.** Common modern misapplication as divine-prosperity promise. Letter-opening convention, not theological thesis. Note for historical-grammatical accuracy.
- **Demetrius's identification.** The letter-carrier? A commended traveling teacher? A local figure? Text is open; surface the possibilities briefly.
