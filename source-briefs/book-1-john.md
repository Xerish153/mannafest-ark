## 1. Book identity

1 John is the NT's most distilled meditation on Christian assurance. Written by the aged apostle John to churches in Asia Minor (late 1st century, likely from Ephesus), the letter addresses a crisis: early docetic / proto-Gnostic teachers were denying that Jesus came "in the flesh" (1 John 4:2–3), hollowing out the incarnation and separating ethical life from doctrinal confession. John responds with three recurring tests of genuine faith — walking in the **light** (1 John 1:5–2:11), loving one another in **love** (2:7–11, 3:11–18, 4:7–21), and confessing Jesus in **truth** / believing on the Son of God for **life** (2:22–23, 4:2, 5:1–13).

The three tests interweave throughout — John returns to each multiple times rather than presenting them sequentially. The letter's Greek is simple (deceptively so), its theology deep, and its pastoral purpose explicit: "these things have I written unto you that believe on the name of the Son of God; that ye may know that ye have eternal life" (1 John 5:13).

1 John is the NT's clearest text on the nature of God (1 John 4:8 "God is love"; 1 John 1:5 "God is light"; 1 John 5:20 "the true God, and eternal life") and one of its most important texts on the atonement (1 John 2:2, 4:10 — propitiation).

Tone: pastoral, textually anchored, theologically careful. Much shorter book than Hebrews — the hub's framework can be tighter.

## 3. Signature verse

**Primary:** 1 John 4:8 — "He that loveth not knoweth not God; for God is love."

This is the letter's most-quoted single line and one of the NT's five most-cited verses generally. Short enough to render in a single panel without truncation.

**Alternative:** 1 John 5:13 — "These things have I written unto you that believe on the name of the Son of God; that ye may know that ye have eternal life." Captures the pastoral purpose of the letter explicitly.

Default to 1 John 4:8 for its theological weight and memorability.

## 4. Stat strip

- **Author:** the apostle John (traditional), same author as the Gospel of John and 2/3 John. Internal evidence supports common authorship; early church unanimous.
- **Approximate date:** late 1st century, c. AD 85–95. Likely written from Ephesus.
- **Audience:** churches in Asia Minor under threat from early docetic / proto-Gnostic teachers denying Christ's true incarnation.
- **Theme (one line):** "Three tests for assurance — light, love, life."
- **Chapter count:** 5.
- **Distinguishing notes:** epistolary but without conventional letter greetings; doesn't name sender or recipient by name; themes interweave rather than proceed linearly.

## 5. Depth 2 framework sections

1. **The crisis — docetic / proto-Gnostic denial of the incarnation** — the letter's occasion. Teachers claiming special knowledge, denying Jesus' true humanity, separating doctrinal confession from ethical obligation. Surface briefly from PD historical sources (Irenaeus *Against Heresies*, PD ANF).
2. **The Light test (1:5–2:11)** — walking in the light, confession of sin, loving the brother. Tension between "we have no sin" (claimed by the opponents) and John's insistence that believers do sin, confess, and are cleansed.
3. **The Love test (2:7–11, 3:11–18, 4:7–21)** — new commandment and old; love for brother as evidence of knowing God; the Christological grounding of Christian love ("he laid down his life for us"); love casting out fear.
4. **The Life / Truth test (2:18–27, 4:1–6, 5:1–13)** — confessing Jesus Christ come in the flesh; the spirit of antichrist; testing the spirits; the witness of Spirit / water / blood; eternal life in the Son.
5. **Assurance (5:13–21)** — the letter's stated purpose. Boldness in prayer; the sin unto death; the elect lady's children; keeping oneself from idols (letter's striking close).

## 6. Chapter summary guidance (5-chapter AI draft queue)

- **Note interweaving pattern** — summaries of chapters 1–5 all acknowledge that the three tests interweave; none of the chapters is cleanly "the light chapter" or "the love chapter."
- **Chapter 2 specifically** — surfaces "little children" / "young men" / "fathers" triads (2:12–14) and introduces "antichrists" (2:18). Summary notes both.
- **Chapter 3 specifically** — "children of God" contrast with "children of the devil" (3:10); love for the brother made concrete (3:17 "whoso hath this world's good, and seeth his brother have need").
- **Chapter 4 specifically** — testing the spirits (4:1–6); God is love (4:7–21). Summary notes both.
- **Chapter 5 specifically** — assurance (5:13); the sin unto death (5:16–17); the Comma Johanneum textual issue (5:7–8) — summary flags this briefly as a textual-criticism note, directs to §10 for detail.
- **Avoid Calvinist / Arminian adjudication** on perseverance passages (5:16–18).

## 10. Known contested points (surface honestly)

1. **The Comma Johanneum (1 John 5:7–8).** The "three that bear record in heaven, the Father, the Word, and the Holy Ghost" clause is absent from almost all Greek manuscripts before the 14th century. Present in the Vulgate tradition, preserved in KJV translation, excluded from most modern critical texts. §7.9 treatment: present the textual evidence, note the KJV-preservation position, note the critical-text position, do not adjudicate. Pastor Marc's drawer may take a position.
2. **The "sin unto death" (1 John 5:16–17).** Readings vary: (a) physical death as divine discipline (1 Cor 11:30 parallel), (b) apostasy / final rejection of Christ, (c) a specific unforgivable sin. Present the readings; don't adjudicate.
3. **Perfectionism vs. sin reality.** 1 John 3:6 ("whosoever abideth in him sinneth not") and 3:9 ("whosoever is born of God doth not commit sin") appear in tension with 1 John 1:8 ("if we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves"). Traditional resolutions: habitual-vs-occasional sin distinction; positional-vs-experiential distinction. Surface the tension honestly, present traditional resolutions.
4. **Antichrist identification.** Some readings take "antichrist" as the docetic teachers John addresses; others take it as a specific eschatological figure (often drawing on 2 Thess 2); others as both. Present without adjudicating.
5. **Authorship (minor — traditional view near-unanimous in PD scholarship).** 2nd-c. AD church attribution to the apostle John is strong; modern critical scholarship occasionally proposes a "Johannine community" composite author. Note briefly; default to traditional attribution.
