## 1. Book identity

Nahum prophesies against Nineveh approximately a century after Jonah's successful mission — the Assyrian capital's repentance proved temporary, and now final judgment is announced. The book is a single sustained oracle: Nineveh, proud, bloody, the destroyer of nations, will herself be destroyed. The prophecy was fulfilled in 612 BC when Babylon and Media sacked Nineveh and the city was erased from human memory until 19th-century archaeology rediscovered it.

Nahum's theological achievement is holding together apparently opposed divine attributes: "The LORD is slow to anger, and great in power, and will not at all acquit the wicked" (1:3) alongside "The LORD is good, a strong hold in the day of trouble; and he knoweth them that trust in him" (1:7). God's wrath toward oppressors is of a piece with his refuge-giving toward the oppressed. The book offers no easy reconciliation — it offers both simultaneously.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Nah 1:7 is the book's pastoral anchor — the refuge-in-trouble promise that balances the harder chapters 2–3 of Nineveh's destruction. Short, memorable, quotable in its own right.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** Nahum the Elkoshite (location of Elkosh uncertain)
- **Date:** c. 663–612 BC — after Thebes' fall (mentioned in 3:8 as past) and before Nineveh's destruction (which the book anticipates)
- **Audience:** Judah, in the shadow of Assyrian threat
- **Theme:** the LORD is good, and he will judge the oppressor
- **Distinguishing notes:** single sustained oracle; acrostic opening (partial, chapter 1); historically verified prophecy of Nineveh's 612 BC fall

## 5. Chapter summary guidance

- Ch 1: the LORD's character — jealous, avenging, slow to anger, good. Partial acrostic. The theological foundation for the oracle.
- Ch 2: siege of Nineveh — vivid imagery of the city's fall, chariots, lions' den emptied.
- Ch 3: woe to the bloody city — catalog of Assyrian cruelty and Nineveh's coming shame; Thebes-comparison (3:8–10) as historical precedent.

## 9. Known contested points

- **Date range within 663–612 BC.** Narrower dating proposals (Ashurbanipal's death c. 627, Josiah's reform era) but none decisive. Note the range; don't pin.
- **Partial acrostic in ch 1.** Chapter 1 contains an incomplete alphabetic acrostic (aleph through kaph is clear; post-kaph is disputed). Some scholars argue text corruption; others argue intentional incompleteness. Minor point; surface only if useful in a chapter summary.
- **Elkosh's location.** Candidates: Galilee (tradition connecting to Capernaum = "Kfar Nahum" = "Nahum's village"), Assyria (Alqush site in modern northern Iraq), Judah. Unresolved; note briefly.
