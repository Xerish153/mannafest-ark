## 1. Book identity

Revelation is the canon's capstone and its most interpretively contested book. John's visions from Patmos — the letters to seven churches, the throne room, the scroll with seven seals, the seven trumpets, the woman and the dragon, the beast and the false prophet, Babylon's fall, Armageddon, the millennial reign, the great white throne, and the new heavens and new earth — have produced more competing interpretive frameworks than any other canonical book.

Four major interpretive schools divide its readership: **Preterist** (most or all of Revelation fulfilled in 1st-c. events, especially AD 70), **Futurist** (most of Revelation awaits end-times fulfillment — dominant dispensational reading), **Historicist** (Revelation describes church-historical eras progressing toward Christ's return — dominant through the Reformation, associated with Reformed tradition), **Idealist / Spiritual** (Revelation depicts timeless conflict between Christ and evil, not chronologically locatable — growing in Reformed/amill circles). Each school has PD defenders: Preterist — David Chilton (copyrighted, living-adjacent), Gentry (living); Futurist — Seiss (PD), Scofield (PD); Historicist — Alford (PD), Barnes (PD); Idealist — Hendriksen (copyrighted). §7.9 discipline throughout.

The book also carries the clearest NT teaching on seven churches (Rev 2–3, cross-linking directly to `/featured/seven-churches` in Wave F), the tree of life restored (Rev 22:2, cross-linking to `/featured/trees`), the new Jerusalem (Rev 21, cross-linking to `/featured/creation-to-new-creation`), and the blood of the Lamb (Rev 5, 7, 12, cross-linking to `/featured/scarlet-thread` and `/title/lamb-of-god`).

Revelation is the Wave C book that most requires editorial discipline. Site presents schools; site does not adjudicate; Pastor Marc's drawer may take positions — and on this book, the drawer is load-bearing.

Tone: careful, multi-school, textually anchored, willing to name what's uncertain. Pastor Marc's editorial voice is most needed here of any book in Wave C.

## 3. Signature verse

**Primary:** Rev 1:7 — "Behold, he cometh with clouds; and every eye shall see him..."

This verse captures the book's eschatological center of gravity in a single line. All four interpretive schools accept the verse's reference to Christ's return (though they differ on timing and circumstances).

**Alternative:** Rev 22:20 — "Surely I come quickly. Amen. Even so, come, Lord Jesus." The book's closing prayer, pastorally resonant.

Default to Rev 1:7 for its Christological-return emphasis.

## 4. Stat strip

- **Author:** John (traditional — internal self-identification as "John" four times in chapter 1; early church attribution to the apostle John, same author as the Gospel). Some modern critical scholarship distinguishes "John of Patmos" from the apostle John; traditional attribution stands on the site.
- **Approximate date:** late 1st century — two traditional dates. **Late-date (c. AD 95–96)** under Domitian's persecution (Irenaeus's attribution, dominant traditional view). **Early-date (c. AD 65–68)** before Jerusalem's fall (required by most preterist readings). Site presents both; most PD commentary assumes late-date.
- **Audience:** seven specific churches in Asia Minor (Rev 1:4, 2–3) and the wider church.
- **Theme (one line):** "The Lamb who was slain reigns."
- **Chapter count:** 22.
- **Distinguishing notes:** the NT's only fully apocalyptic book; heavy use of symbolic imagery; structural reliance on sevens; extensive OT allusion (especially Daniel, Ezekiel, Isaiah, Psalms).

## 5. Depth 2 framework sections

1. **Occasion and structure** — John on Patmos (Rev 1:9), the commission to write to seven churches, the book's sevens-based literary structure, its debt to Daniel's apocalyptic tradition. Date debate surfaced here (late-date Domitian vs early-date Nero).
2. **Seven churches (Rev 2–3)** — the letters to Ephesus, Smyrna, Pergamos, Thyatira, Sardis, Philadelphia, Laodicea. Cross-link directly to `/featured/seven-churches` (Wave F shipped) — this is the book hub's deepest cross-surface integration.
3. **Throne room and the Lamb (Rev 4–5)** — the transition from earthly correspondence to heavenly vision; the scroll and the slain Lamb who is worthy to open it.
4. **Seven seals, seven trumpets, seven bowls (Rev 6–16)** — the three sevens of judgment. Interpretive schools surveyed here §7.9:
   - Preterist (all three sevens fulfilled in AD 66–70 Jewish War)
   - Futurist (all three sevens describe future tribulation; dispensational chronology usually subdivides)
   - Historicist (the sevens span church history, from apostolic era to Reformation to end)
   - Idealist (the sevens depict recurring patterns of divine judgment and deliverance across all ages)
5. **The woman, the dragon, the beast (Rev 12–14)** — cosmic conflict surfaced. Cross-link to `/featured/seed-promise/revelation-12-woman-and-dragon`. Interpretive schools differ on whether the beast figures are historical (Nero per preterist, various Roman emperors per historicist) or future (dispensational Antichrist).
6. **Babylon, Armageddon, and the millennium (Rev 17–20)** — perhaps the most divisive section. Dispensational premillennial (literal 1,000-year reign after Christ's return), historic premillennial (similar but without rapture theology), amillennial (figurative thousand years = church age), postmillennial (gospel triumph produces a golden age before Christ returns). **All four millennial positions surfaced at depth 2** with their strongest defenders; site does not adjudicate; Pastor Marc's drawer may.
7. **New heavens and new earth (Rev 21–22)** — the book's resolution. Cross-link to `/featured/creation-to-new-creation` and `/featured/trees/tree-of-life-in-revelation`. This section is less interpretively divisive than the preceding chapters — most schools agree on the final vision's meaning even if differing on what precedes it.

- **Avoid AD-date fixing** — no summary states "fulfilled in AD 70" or "will occur in 20XX."

## 8. Commentary tradition priority

- **Evangelical / Dispensational — Seiss's *Apocalypse*** (PD, 3 volumes). Futurist interpretive school primary voice; excellent PD treatment.
- **Reformed — Calvin did not write on Revelation.** Use **Matthew Henry on Revelation** (PD) as Reformed/Puritan voice; **Alford's Greek Testament** (PD) as Anglican-Academic voice with historicist sympathies.
- **Patristic — Chrysostom did not write on Revelation extensively.** Use **Augustine's *City of God* book 20** (PD NPNF series 1) for the amillennial / Augustinian reading on Rev 20's millennium.
- **Dispensational — Scofield Reference Bible notes** (1909 PD edition). Represents early-20th-c. dispensational futurism.
- **Historicist — Barnes on Revelation** (PD). Represents 19th-c. Reformed historicism.
- **Evangelical / Contested readings — Bullinger on Revelation** (PD). Some of Bullinger's readings are contested even within dispensational circles — surface carefully.

## 10. Known contested points (surface honestly)

1. **Date of composition.** Late-date AD 95 (Domitian, Irenaeus's attribution, mainstream traditional view) vs. early-date AD 65–68 (Nero, required by preterist readings). Present both; most PD commentary assumes late-date.
2. **Four interpretive schools.** Preterist / Futurist / Historicist / Idealist — named in §5 framework section 4. Each presented with strongest PD defenders.
3. **Millennial views.** Premillennial (historic or dispensational), amillennial, postmillennial. Presented in §5 framework section 6. Pastor Marc's drawer may take a position.
4. **The beast's identification (Rev 13:18, 666).** Nero Caesar (preterist, via Hebrew gematria of "Neron Kaisar"), Roman empire (historicist), a future Antichrist figure (futurist), symbol of all anti-Christian power (idealist). Present; don't identify.
5. **Babylon (Rev 17–18).** Rome (strong patristic tradition), Jerusalem (some preterists), apostate church (some historicists), end-times literal Babylon rebuilt (some dispensationalists). Present the top candidates; don't endorse.
6. **Rapture doctrine.** Pre-tribulation (dispensational, Darby), mid-tribulation, post-tribulation, pre-wrath, historic premillennial (no "rapture" distinct from return). Surface briefly at Rev 4:1 (the "come up hither") section where the pre-trib reading anchors; do not adjudicate.
7. **The two witnesses (Rev 11).** Moses + Elijah / Enoch + Elijah / church in testimony / literal individuals yet to appear — multiple readings; present without endorsing.
8. **144,000 (Rev 7 and 14).** Literal Jewish remnant (dispensational), symbolic for the whole redeemed church (amill / historicist), specific ethnic-Israel remnant at the end (some premill). Present.
9. **The millennium (Rev 20:1–6).** Literal 1,000-year reign after Christ's return (premillennial), figurative thousand years = church age (amillennial), golden age before Christ's return produced by gospel triumph (postmillennial). **The single most interpretively-loaded passage in the canon.** Present all three with their strongest PD defenders; Pastor Marc's drawer may take a position.
