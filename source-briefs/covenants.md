## 1. Page identity

The Hebrew idiom for making a covenant is *karath berith* — literally, "to cut a covenant." The grammar is physical: parties walking between the pieces of bisected animals, invoking on themselves the fate of the pieces if they break the oath (Gen 15; Jer 34:18–20). The Bible's story is organized around these cuttings. Adamic. Noahic. Abrahamic. Mosaic. Davidic. New. Six covenants in succession, cumulative rather than replacing, each narrowing the line of promise while widening the scope of fulfillment.

The page presents the six as a visible sequence, anchors each to its cutting-moment, shows the parties and terms, and traces the thread that ties them together — a line running from the first promise of a seed who crushes the serpent to the new heart written on flesh.

Covenant framework is a contested interpretive field. Reformed theology reads covenant as Scripture's master category (covenant of redemption, covenant of works, covenant of grace). Dispensational theology reads covenant as sequential and distinct with Israel-Church distinction preserved. The site presents the six historical covenants as the common inheritance of both traditions, surfaces the framework debates honestly, and lets the student see where the disagreement lies rather than hiding it.

Tone: structural, visible grammar, narrative flow. Not polemical.

## 3. Depth 2 framework sections

### Section 1 — *Karath berith*: covenant-cutting in ANE practice and in Scripture

- The verb *karath* (to cut) + *berith* (covenant) as the standard Hebrew idiom. The cutting language refers to the bisected-animal ceremony (Gen 15:9–18; Jer 34:18–20) in which the parties pass between the pieces, invoking on themselves the fate of the animals if they break oath.
- **Gen 15 as structural key:** Abraham bisects the animals, a "smoking furnace and burning lamp" (representing God) passes between them — Abraham does not. The one-sided passage is theologically load-bearing. Abraham does not obligate himself; God obligates himself alone. This is the grammatical basis for calling the Abrahamic unconditional.
- ANE background: covenant-cutting is widely attested in second-millennium BC Hittite and Mesopotamian treaty practice. Source: Meredith Kline's *Treaty of the Great King* tradition (Kline living/recent; cite only) or PD-safe alternatives (Keil & Delitzsch on Gen 15, PD).
- **Jer 34:18–20** — the judgment against Judah for breaking the release-of-slaves covenant is framed explicitly as: "I will give the men that have transgressed my covenant... which passed between the parts of the calf." The ancient meaning is still alive at Jeremiah's time.

### Section 2 — The six covenants surveyed

Each covenant treated briefly at depth 2; fuller treatment in drilldowns.

**Adamic covenant** (Gen 1:26–30; Gen 2:15–17; Gen 3:14–19 post-fall addendum).
- Parties: God with Adam (representing humanity).
- Terms: dominion + prohibition on tree of knowledge; post-fall, pain / toil / mortality + promise of seed (3:15).
- Conditional or unconditional: contested. Reformed "covenant of works" reads it as conditional-and-broken; dispensationalists sometimes treat Gen 1–2 as Edenic (pre-fall) and Gen 3:14–19 as Adamic (post-fall). The site surfaces both readings.

**Noahic covenant** (Gen 8:20–9:17).
- Parties: God with Noah and all flesh.
- Terms: never again a flood to destroy the earth; seasons preserved; capital-punishment institution; blessing to be fruitful.
- Sign: rainbow.
- Conditional or unconditional: unconditional in its preservation-promise aspect. The most universal of the covenants — "with every living creature."

**Abrahamic covenant** (Gen 12:1–3; 15:1–21; 17:1–22; 22:15–18).
- Parties: God with Abraham and his seed.
- Terms: land, descendants, blessing to the nations.
- Sign: circumcision.
- Unconditional — the Gen 15 one-sided passage establishes this. God's single obligation.
- Galatians 3's argument: the Abrahamic covenant precedes the Mosaic by 430 years and cannot be set aside by the later one.

**Mosaic covenant** (Ex 19–24; Deut 28–30).
- Parties: God with national Israel.
- Terms: the Decalogue + the ceremonial and civil law + blessings and curses for obedience.
- Sign: Sabbath.
- **Conditional.** This is the one conditional covenant. Obedience brings blessing; disobedience brings curse. Jer 31:32 explicitly notes this conditionality when contrasted with the New Covenant.

**Davidic covenant** (2 Sam 7:8–17; 1 Chr 17:7–15; Ps 89; Ps 132).
- Parties: God with David and his dynastic line.
- Terms: an everlasting house, throne, and kingdom; David's seed to sit on the throne forever.
- Sign: the throne.
- Unconditional. Ps 89:30–37 explicitly notes that David's sons' disobedience brings discipline but does not break the covenant.

**New covenant** (Jer 31:31–34; Ezek 36:26–27; Heb 8:6–13; Heb 10:15–18).
- Parties: God with "the house of Israel and the house of Judah" (Jer 31:31, expanded in Pauline theology to the Gentiles grafted in per Rom 11).
- Terms: law written on the heart; knowledge of God direct; sins remembered no more.
- Sign: bread and cup (Luke 22:20).
- Unconditional from God's side; inaugurated at the Last Supper; ratified at the cross; consummated at Christ's return.

### Section 3 — Conditional vs. unconditional classification

Four unconditional (Noahic, Abrahamic, Davidic, New) + one conditional (Mosaic) + one contested (Adamic, classified differently by tradition).

- The classification is theologically load-bearing. The unconditional covenants ground divine promise in divine oath-keeping; the conditional covenant tests human oath-keeping and finds it lacking, setting up the need for the New Covenant.
- **Reformed framework** reads the unconditional covenants as "covenant of grace" in successive administrations; the conditional Mosaic as "covenant of works" republished to show inability. Sources: Westminster Confession ch. 7 (PD), John Owen (PD).
- **Dispensational framework** keeps each covenant distinct, preserves Israel-Church distinction, reads the Abrahamic land-promise as literal-territorial and awaiting future fulfillment. Sources: Scofield Reference Bible 1909 (PD), Lewis Sperry Chafer (living-work-status complex; cite only safer).

### Section 4 — The covenant of redemption (pre-temporal, Reformed-specific)

- Reformed tradition posits a pre-temporal *pactum salutis* among the persons of the Trinity: the Father gives a people to the Son, the Son undertakes redemption, the Spirit applies it. Sources: John Owen, *The Death of Death in the Death of Christ* (PD); Herman Witsius (PD).
- Dispensational and non-Reformed evangelical traditions typically do not affirm the covenant of redemption as a distinct category.
- **Surface per §7.9 opposing-views discipline:** this is a Reformed theological framework, not a pan-Christian consensus. Not site-endorsed; presented as a framework a student will encounter in Reformed literature.

### Section 5 — The New Covenant as fulfillment

- **Jer 31:31–34** — the explicit promise of a new covenant with Israel and Judah, with the distinguishing feature that it is inward (law on the heart) not merely outward (stone tablets).
- **Ezek 36:26–27** — paired prophecy of heart-of-flesh replacing heart-of-stone and Spirit-giving.
- **Luke 22:20** — Jesus' inauguration: "This cup is the new testament in my blood."
- **Heb 8:6–13** — the Hebrews-author's direct exposition, quoting Jer 31 in full and arguing the new supersedes the old.
- **Heb 10:15–18** — the completion-of-sacrifice argument: "their sins and iniquities will I remember no more."
- The typological trajectory: every prior covenant pointed forward to this one. Adamic (seed promise) → Noahic (preservation through judgment) → Abrahamic (blessing to nations) → Mosaic (righteousness-needed-and-found-lacking) → Davidic (throne for the seed) → New (heart transformation). The structural argument is cumulative, not replacive.

## 10. Known contested points

1. **Adamic classification.** Reformed ("covenant of works") vs. dispensational (Edenic + Adamic as two) vs. those who deny an explicit Adamic covenant altogether (Gen 1–3 as creation-order rather than covenant). Surface all three.

2. **Covenant of redemption** (pre-temporal) — Reformed-specific. Not affirmed by all traditions. Section 4 surfaces honestly.

3. **Israel-Church relationship** — covenant theology tends toward continuity; dispensationalism preserves distinction. The page does not adjudicate; Rom 11 cross-reference lets the student work it out.

4. **Whether the Abrahamic land-promise is literal-territorial or typological-fulfilled-in-Christ.** Dispensational literal reading vs. Reformed christological-typological reading. Surface both; no site adjudication.

5. **Replacement vs. fulfillment.** Whether the New Covenant *replaces* Israel / the Mosaic (supersessionism, often critiqued) or *fulfills* (typological continuity). Rom 11's olive-tree imagery is the hermeneutical key; the site anchors there without adjudicating the broader systematic-theology question.

6. **Gen 1:26–30 as covenant or creation-mandate.** Whether the dominion-charge constitutes a covenant-of-works (Reformed) or a creation-ordinance not yet covenantal (some dispensationalists).
