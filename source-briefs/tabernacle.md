## Page intent

The most architecturally complete typology in scripture. Every measurement, every material, every piece of furniture, every woven thread carries deliberate meaning. Hebrews 8–10 explicitly identifies the entire structure as "a copy and shadow of heavenly things" and walks through how Christ fulfills each piece.

The aesthetic thesis: a top-down floor plan as the depth-1 visual, every furniture element directly clickable. No other feature page on the site renders as a floor plan. The Tabernacle owns that visual identity.

Editorial weight: this is the page that fulfills Doctrine B §7.2's worked example. The doctrine has been waiting for this page.

The structural logic that makes the page teach: the Tabernacle's spatial layout is the gospel acted out. A sinner enters at the east gate, encounters the Bronze Altar (sacrifice for sin) and the Bronze Laver (cleansing), then the priestly mediator carries that work into the Holy Place (light, bread, prayer), and finally — once a year — the High Priest passes through the veil into the Most Holy Place to make atonement at the Mercy Seat. The veil is torn at Christ's death (Matt 27:51); all believers now have direct access (Heb 10:19–20). The floor plan is a literal map of redemption.

Distinct from Scarlet Thread (the *ritual* that happened inside) and Bronze Serpent (single event): this page is about the *space itself* and the *gospel encoded in its architecture*.

## Depth 2 — Framework

Six framework sections.

### 2.1 The three zones — three depths of access

Walks the spatial logic of the structure as the gospel narrative.

Content:
- **Outer Court (100 × 50 cubits)** — open to any Israelite. Bronze metals throughout. The Bronze Altar (sacrifice) and Bronze Laver (cleansing) stand here. Bronze symbolizes judgment and sin met head-on.
- **Holy Place (20 × 10 × 10 cubits)** — entry restricted to priests. Gold replaces bronze. The Table of Showbread (life), Golden Lampstand (light), and Altar of Incense (prayer) form the daily worship of the priesthood.
- **Most Holy Place (10 × 10 × 10 cubits — a perfect cube)** — entered only by the High Priest, only on the Day of Atonement, only with sacrificial blood. The Ark of the Covenant rests here, with the Mercy Seat as its lid. God's manifest presence dwells above the Mercy Seat between the cherubim.

Each zone is *more restricted, more golden, more glorious* than the one before. The structure teaches: there are degrees of access to God, and that access requires sacrifice and mediation. The veil between Holy Place and Most Holy Place is the system's central limit. The Gospels record that veil tearing top-to-bottom the moment Christ died (Matt 27:51, Mark 15:38, Luke 23:45). The system's central limit is removed by Christ.

Visual: a small annotated cross-section diagram showing the three zones in scale, with the relative cubit dimensions called out, and the veil clearly marked as the boundary that gets torn.

One sentence; centuries of Christological exposition.

Founder editorial note opportunity here: the wood-and-gold construction is one of the most beautiful pieces of typology in scripture; rich ground for a Pastor Marc framing.

### 2.3 The Hebrews 8–10 framework — the inspired commentary

Dedicated framework section. Hebrews chapters 8–10 are essentially an extended sermon on the Tabernacle's typology. Where Typology of Christ §2.6 covers Hebrews at the hub level (general typological frame), this page walks Hebrews 8–10 specifically through the Tabernacle's spatial logic.

Content cards:

- **Heb 8:1–6 — "the true tabernacle"** — The earthly Tabernacle is a copy of a heavenly reality. Christ is now ministering in the true tabernacle, of which the wilderness one was a shadow.
- **Heb 9:1–10 — the spatial walkthrough** — Hebrews itself walks the Tabernacle floor plan, naming each room and key piece of furniture (lampstand, table of showbread, altar of incense, ark, mercy seat). The author treats the floor plan as theology in space.
- **Heb 9:11–14 — the High Priest enters the greater Tabernacle** — Christ enters a greater and more perfect Tabernacle "not made with hands," with his own blood, obtaining eternal redemption.
- **Heb 9:24 — Christ enters heaven itself** — The Most Holy Place was a *figure of the true*. Christ has entered the actual presence of God on our behalf.
- **Heb 10:19–20 — the veil = Christ's flesh** — *"Having therefore, brethren, boldness to enter into the holiest by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way, which he hath consecrated for us, through the veil, that is to say, his flesh."* The single most theologically loaded verse on the page. The veil is identified as Christ's flesh; torn flesh = open access to God.

Cross-link card to Typology of Christ §2.6 for general Hebrews framing.

### 2.4 The cube — Most Holy Place to New Jerusalem

Featured spotlight card. One of the most aesthetically striking typological observations in the Bible: a single architectural shape ties the Pentateuch to Revelation.

Content:
- **The Most Holy Place (Ex 26)** — a perfect cube, 10 × 10 × 10 cubits (~15ft × 15ft × 15ft). God's dwelling place on earth.
- **Solomon's Temple Holy of Holies (1 Kings 6:20)** — also a perfect cube, 20 × 20 × 20 cubits. Same shape, larger scale.
- **The New Jerusalem (Rev 21:16)** — *"the length and the breadth and the height of it are equal."* 12,000 furlongs on each side — a cube ~1,400 miles per side. Same shape, vastly larger scale. The entire city is the Most Holy Place, the dwelling place of God with humanity.

The continuity:
- The Wilderness Tabernacle's Holy of Holies — God dwelling with one nation, behind a veil, restricted to one man one day a year
- Solomon's Temple — God dwelling with one nation, behind a veil, restricted as before, in a fixed location
- The New Jerusalem — God dwelling with all his people, no veil, no temple at all because *"the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are the temple of it"* (Rev 21:22)

The entire arc of redemption can be read in the geometry: same shape, growing scale, decreasing restriction. The Tabernacle's tiny cube is the prototype of the eternal dwelling.

Visual: a small comparison diagram showing the three cubes in scale silhouette — Tabernacle Most Holy Place at one end, Solomon's Temple HOH in the middle, New Jerusalem dwarfing both. Captions per cube.

Strong founder editorial hook here. The cube continuity is the kind of observation that earns founder voice.

### 2.5 The Day of Atonement — what happened inside

Brief framework card. The Tabernacle's central annual event is Yom Kippur (Lev 16). The High Priest enters the Most Holy Place, sprinkles blood on the Mercy Seat, makes atonement for the nation. The whole structure exists for this one day.

Content:
- One sentence per ritual element: high priest enters; blood is applied to Mercy Seat; scapegoat sent into wilderness bearing sins; people forgiven for another year
- Cross-link to **Scarlet Thread §2.2** for the full treatment of the Talmud Yoma 39b evidence (the scarlet thread that turned white until AD 30) — the Tabernacle is the physical setting for that ritual
- Cross-link to **Typology of Christ §2.5** Feasts category — Day of Atonement entry

Single card; minimal duplication. The full Yom Kippur treatment lives at Scarlet Thread; this card is the *spatial context* — the Most Holy Place is *where* the ritual happens.

### 2.6 The pattern shown on the mountain

Short framework card on Exodus 25:9, 25:40, 26:30, 27:8 — Moses is repeatedly told to build the Tabernacle "according to the pattern shewed thee in the mount." The Tabernacle is not improvised; it is a deliberate replica of something Moses was shown.

Hebrews 8:5 picks this up and tells us what Moses saw: the heavenly reality of which the earthly Tabernacle is a shadow. The Tabernacle is, in effect, an architectural blueprint of heaven itself, scaled for human eyes.

Single card. Editorial framing for why every detail matters: if the structure is a divine blueprint, the dimensions, materials, and arrangements are not decorative — they are revelation in three dimensions.

### Special drilldowns

**Mercy Seat (kapporet)** — extended treatment:
- Hebrew **kapporet** comes from the verb **kaphar** ("to cover" / "to atone"). The Greek translators in the Septuagint chose **hilastērion** for it.
- **Romans 3:25** uses the *exact same Greek word* (hilastērion) of Christ: *"whom God hath set forth to be a propitiation [hilastērion] through faith in his blood."* Paul deliberately identifies Christ as the Mercy Seat itself — the place where God's wrath is satisfied and atonement is made.
- This single word-link is one of the most explicit OT-NT terminological identifications in scripture. Worth the spotlight.
- Featured commentary voice for this drilldown: **John Owen**, *The Death of Death in the Death of Christ* (1647, fully PD) — the classic Reformed treatment of propitiation, with extensive Mercy Seat exposition

**The Veil (paroket)** — extended treatment:
- The veil was woven of blue, purple, scarlet, and fine linen, with cherubim embroidered on it (Ex 26:31). The cherubim are the same beings that guard the way to the tree of life in Eden (Gen 3:24).
- The Eden parallel: cherubim guarding access to God in Eden; cherubim woven into the veil guarding access to God in the Tabernacle. The veil is, in one reading, a reminder of the Eden separation.
- **Matthew 27:51** — *"the veil of the temple was rent in twain from the top to the bottom."* Top to bottom signals divine action (no human could tear from the top of a veil ~60 feet high in the Second Temple).
- **Hebrews 10:19–20** — the veil is identified as Christ's flesh. Torn flesh = open access. The Eden separation is reversed.
- Featured commentary: **Charles Spurgeon**, "The Rent Veil" (sermon, 1888, PD) — the standard evangelical exposition

**Ark of the Covenant** — extended treatment:
- Construction: acacia wood overlaid with gold, inside and out (Ex 25:11). The wood-and-gold motif at its purest expression.
- Contents (per Heb 9:4): the tablets of the Covenant (the Law), the golden pot of manna, Aaron's rod that budded. Each item is itself typological:
  - **Tablets** — Christ fulfilled the Law (Matt 5:17)
  - **Manna** — Christ is the bread from heaven (John 6:31–35)
  - **Aaron's rod that budded** — Christ's resurrection from death to life (Num 17:8; cf. Acts 2:24)
- The Ark itself disappears from history after Jerusalem's fall to Babylon (587 BC). It is *not* in the Second Temple. Yet **Revelation 11:19** records it being seen in the heavenly temple at the end: *"there was seen in his temple the ark of his testament."*

**Bronze Altar** — note on dimensions:
- 5 × 5 × 3 cubits (Ex 27:1) — a square base, lower in height. Designed for an animal to be lifted onto and consumed by fire. Cross-link to **Bronze Serpent** brief on bronze metal symbolism.

**Lampstand (Menorah)** — note on construction:
- Hammered from a single talent of pure gold (Ex 25:31, 36) — ~75 pounds of gold, formed by beating from one solid piece. Christ's body, formed for one purpose, made the source of light. Cross-link to **Revelation 1:12–13** where Christ stands among seven golden lampstands (the seven churches).
