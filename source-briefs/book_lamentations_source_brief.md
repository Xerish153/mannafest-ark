## 1. Book identity

Five poems of grief over Jerusalem's destruction in 586 BC, traditionally attributed to Jeremiah — the prophet present at the catastrophe. Four of the five chapters are alphabetic acrostics, each verse beginning with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet. Chapter 3, the theological center, triples the acrostic (three verses per letter), and at its structural midpoint places the book's hope passage: "His compassions fail not, they are new every morning" (3:22–23).

The book sits between Jeremiah and Ezekiel in Christian canonical order. In the Hebrew Bible it's among the Writings (Ketuvim), part of the Megillot scroll cluster read on Tisha B'Av — the Jewish fast commemorating the Temple's destruction. The form (acrostic) imposes discipline on the content (grief); the structural center holds out hope without denying the grief around it.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Lam 3:22–23 is the book's theological turning point — hope in the center of grief, form finding a middle that holds. Anchors the hymn "Great Is Thy Faithfulness." Two verses fit the signature panel without truncation.
