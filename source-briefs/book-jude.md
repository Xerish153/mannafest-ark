## 1. Book identity

Jude is a 25-verse single-chapter letter from "Jude, the servant of Jesus Christ, and brother of James" (1:1). Traditional identification of the author: Jude the half-brother of Jesus, the same Jude (Judas) listed alongside James in the gospel accounts of Jesus' brothers (Matt 13:55, Mark 6:3). Date: c. 65–80 AD, though dating is debated alongside the question of Jude's relationship to 2 Peter.

Jude states his shift of subject explicitly. He had intended to write "of the common salvation" (1:3) — a general theological letter — but found it "needful" to instead urge readers to "earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered unto the saints." The remainder of the letter is a polemic against false teachers who have crept in unawares, turning grace into license and denying the LORD. Jude's rhetorical arsenal draws heavily on Jewish tradition: Cain, Balaam, Korah (1:11); fallen angels chained in darkness (1:6); Sodom and Gomorrah (1:7); a dispute between Michael and the devil over Moses's body (1:9, apparently drawing on the *Assumption of Moses*); Enoch's prophecy (1:14–15, explicitly quoting *1 Enoch* 1:9).

Jude's citation of *1 Enoch* in 1:14–15 — "And Enoch also, the seventh from Adam, prophesied of these" — is the NT's only explicit citation of a non-canonical book as prophetic speech. The letter's close (1:24–25, "Now unto him that is able to keep you from falling, and to present you faultless before the presence of his glory with exceeding joy") is one of the NT's great benedictions.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Jude 1:3 is the letter's explicit thesis — "earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered unto the saints." Captures the polemic intent; widely cited; memorable. "Once delivered" (*hapax*, once-for-all) is load-bearing for the historic Protestant understanding of the faith's fixity.

## 9. Known contested points

- **Authorship.** Traditional: Jude, half-brother of Jesus. Alternative (minority): pseudonymous. Most conservative scholarship and church tradition: Jude brother of James. Note the tradition.
- **Date and relationship to 2 Peter.** The Jude–2 Peter literary parallel (Jude 4–18 ≈ 2 Pet 2) points to dependency in one direction; current majority scholarly view: 2 Peter depends on Jude (Jude earlier, c. 65–75; 2 Peter c. 65–68 or later). Traditional: 2 Peter earlier. §7.9 discipline — surface both.
- **Enoch citation status.** Does Jude treat *1 Enoch* as inspired / canonical / prophetic-speech / merely-quoted? Christian tradition has handled this variously. The text says Enoch "prophesied" — grammatically this treats the content as genuine prophecy. Most PD Reformed commentary: Jude quotes a true prophetic utterance preserved (whether originally given through Enoch or preserved elsewhere); this does not make *1 Enoch* as a whole canonical Scripture. Ethiopian Orthodox canon includes *1 Enoch*; all other major Christian canons exclude. §7.9 discipline.
- **Assumption of Moses citation (1:9).** Similar to the Enoch question. Dispute between Michael and Satan over Moses's body appears in the *Assumption of Moses* tradition. Jude draws on the tradition without making the whole tradition canonical. Surface.
- **Canonical reception.** Jude was one of the "antilegomena" (disputed books) in the early church — disputed partly because of the non-canonical citations. Universally accepted by the late 4th century (Athanasius 367, Council of Carthage 397). Note as historical-canon fact.
