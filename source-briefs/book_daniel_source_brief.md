## 1. Book identity

Daniel is Scripture's densest apocalyptic text and one of its most interpretively contested. The first six chapters are court narratives — a Hebrew exile elevated to Babylonian and Persian courts, faithful under three kings, delivered from a furnace and a lions' den. The last six chapters are apocalyptic visions — a succession of world-empires, a Son of Man approaching the Ancient of Days, a 70-weeks calculation locating Messiah in time, a final vision of a great conflict and resurrection.

No book in the canon requires more §7.9 discipline. Interpretive schools divide fundamentally on the composition date, on the identity of the little horn, on the 70-weeks timing, on the king-of-the-north identification, on whether the visions describe the inter-testamental period (Antiochus Epiphanes and the Maccabean era), the Roman empire and Christ's first coming, or the still-future tribulation and second coming. Dispensational, historicist, preterist, amillennial, and premillennial readings all claim Daniel as a primary text.

The hub page presents the text, the historical setting, the major interpretive schools at depth 2, and chapter summaries crisp enough to orient students without choosing a school for them. The Son-of-Man passage (Dan 7:13–14) cross-surfaces into Batch 7-B's `/title/son-of-man`; that's one of the book's clearest NT echoes.

Tone: careful, textually grounded, multi-school. The founder editorial drawer is the only place this page's own voice appears on contested eschatology.

## 3. Signature verse

**Primary:** Dan 7:13–14 (Son of Man before the Ancient of Days). This is Daniel's Christological apex and the most directly NT-citing passage (Jesus uses "Son of Man" more than eighty times; Matt 26:64 quotes Dan 7 directly at Jesus' trial).

**Alternative:** Dan 2:44 (the kingdom that shall never be destroyed).

Default to Dan 7:13–14 for the Son-of-Man bridge into the NT.

## 4. Stat strip

- **Author:** traditionally Daniel (taken by Jesus in Matt 24:15, "spoken of by Daniel the prophet"). Critical scholarship dates composition to the 2nd c. BC Maccabean period — §7.9 discipline: surface both dating positions.
- **Approximate date:** traditional view ~6th c. BC; critical view ~165 BC. Site default uses traditional dating, flags critical view at depth 2.
- **Audience:** Jewish exiles in Babylon; later, Jewish community through the inter-testamental period.
- **Theme (one line):** "A sovereign God in Gentile courts — and a Son of Man coming with clouds."
- **Chapter count:** 12.
- **Distinguishing notes:** bilingual (Heb 1:1–2:4a, 8:1–12:13; Aramaic 2:4b–7:28); mixes court-narrative with apocalyptic vision at the chapter 7 hinge.

## 5. Depth 2 framework sections

1. **Historical setting and bilingualism** — the 605 BC first exile, Daniel's arrival in Babylon, his life spanning the Babylonian-to-Persian transition (Dan 5 → Dan 6), and the book's structurally significant language shift at Dan 2:4b (Hebrew → Aramaic) and back at Dan 8:1 (Aramaic → Hebrew). Sourced from PD historical works (Herodotus, Xenophon's *Cyropaedia* for Persian backgrounds, Berossus fragments).
2. **Court narratives (Dan 1–6)** — faith under pressure across three royal courts (Nebuchadnezzar, Belshazzar, Darius the Mede / Cyrus). Six narrative set-pieces.
3. **Apocalyptic visions (Dan 7–12)** — four beasts, the little horn, the ram and the goat, the 70 weeks, the great vision of Dan 10–12. Literary structure shifts from narrative to apocalyptic.
4. **The 70 weeks (Dan 9:24–27) — interpretive schools surveyed per §7.9:**
   - Dispensational / futurist (Scofield, Ryrie, Walvoord) — 70th week is a still-future tribulation separated by a church-age gap
   - Historic premillennial (Ladd) — 70th week culminates at Christ's return but with less rigid chronology
   - Amillennial (Calvin, Reformed mainstream) — the 69 weeks culminate at Christ, the 70th week encompasses the church age figuratively
   - Preterist — the entire 70 weeks fulfilled by AD 70 and the Temple's destruction
   - Critical (Maccabean) — the 70 weeks are a vaticinium ex eventu from the 2nd c. BC Antiochus Epiphanes crisis
   All five presented with their strongest defenders; none adjudicated.
5. **The Son of Man (Dan 7:13–14)** — the figure "like a son of man" approaching the Ancient of Days, receiving dominion. Jesus' self-designation as "Son of Man" draws from here. Cross-link to `/title/son-of-man`.
6. **The king of the north and the great vision (Dan 10–12)** — the final vision's extreme historical specificity (commonly read as the Ptolemaic-Seleucid wars), the resurrection passage (Dan 12:2), the 1,290-days / 1,335-days calculations. Interpretive schools surveyed briefly; full treatment in chapter summaries and commentary.

## 9. Sourcing caveats

- **Sir Robert Anderson's *The Coming Prince* (1895, PD)** — the classic dispensational treatment of the 70 weeks. Cite for that interpretive school's chronology (Anderson's 173,880-day calculation). PD.
- **No E.W. Bullinger on Daniel reproduction** — Bullinger's *Number in Scripture* (PD) touches Daniel but should be cited carefully; his specific numerical claims are contested.
- **No living-author eschatology teachers reproduced** — Missler, Brewer, Huff, MacArthur, Walvoord (d. 2002, still under copyright), Ryrie (d. 2016), etc. Cite only as pointer where necessary.
- **Herodotus / Xenophon / Berossus for Persian history** — PD classical sources only.
- **Critical dating (Maccabean composition)** — surface the argument via PD critical scholarship (Rowley's 1935 work is past the PD horizon in most jurisdictions; later critical work often copyrighted — paraphrase and cite).

## 10. Known contested points (surface honestly)

1. **Composition date.** 6th c. BC traditional vs 2nd c. BC critical (Maccabean). Jesus' reference to "Daniel the prophet" (Matt 24:15) is the traditional anchor; critical scholarship points to Aramaic linguistic features and historical specificity about the Seleucid period as evidence of later composition. §7.9 discipline.
2. **Darius the Mede identification (Dan 5:31 / 6).** No extrabiblical historical record names "Darius the Mede." Candidates: Ugbaru / Gubaru (governor of Babylon under Cyrus), Cyaxares II, a throne-name for Cyrus himself. Present the problem; note the candidates; do not adjudicate.
3. **The little horn (Dan 7:8, 8:9).** Dan 7's little horn = Dan 8's little horn? Or two different figures? (Antiochus Epiphanes for Dan 8 is widely accepted; Dan 7's figure varies by school.)
4. **Fourth kingdom (Dan 2, 7) identification.** Rome (traditional / dispensational) vs. Greece continued through the successors (some critical readings).
5. **70 weeks.** See §5 framework section 4 — five interpretive schools presented.
6. **Antichrist vs. Antiochus Epiphanes (Dan 11:36–45).** Dispensational reading: the transition mid-chapter from Antiochus to a still-future Antichrist. Preterist/amill: the entire chapter describes Antiochus. Present both.
7. **Dan 9:27 "he shall confirm the covenant."** Who is "he" — Messiah (amill / traditional Reformed) or Antichrist (dispensational)? Grammatical ambiguity is real; surface both readings.
