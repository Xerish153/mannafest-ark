## 1. Page identity

A letter that looks like a cross turns up at every decisive moment of protection, judgment, and redemption in Scripture. The paleo-Hebrew **taw** (ת in its pre-exilic form: a diagonal cross or X) is the word Ezekiel uses for the seal on the foreheads of those spared in chapter 9. The Passover blood is applied in three positions on the doorposts. The Revelation sealing protects the 144,000 from eschatological judgment. The cross is the instrument of the final atonement. The page traces this single visual — a cross-shaped mark — through all four moments and invites the student to consider whether that shape is coincidence or pattern.

This is a page about one letter. The mode is understatement — let the convergence speak.

## 3. Depth 2 framework sections

### Section 1 — Origin of taw in paleo-Hebrew

Before the Babylonian exile (c. 586 BC), Hebrew was written in paleo-Hebrew script, where the letter taw was shaped as a diagonal cross (X) or plus-sign (+). Post-exile, Hebrew adopted the Aramaic-derived square script in which taw takes its current form (ת). The claim is not that "the Hebrew letter has always looked like a cross" — it is that in the script in use at the time of Ezekiel and the Passover institution, taw and cross shared a form.

**Sources:** Gesenius's Hebrew Grammar (PD), Jewish Encyclopedia 1906 (PD) on paleo-Hebrew letterforms.

### Section 2 — The Ezekiel 9 seal

Ezekiel 9 vision: the glory of God prepares to depart the Temple; the man with the writing kit is commanded, "set a mark (`taw`) upon the foreheads of the men that sigh and that cry for all the abominations." The Hebrew word translated "mark" *is taw* — the name of the letter, used as the noun for the sign itself. Those bearing the taw are spared as the executioners begin "at my sanctuary" and move outward.

- Jewish interpretive tradition: Rashi on Ezek 9:4 (Rashi 1040–1105, PD) treats the letter consequentially. The Talmud Shabbat 55a (Rodkinson PD translation) preserves a gemara on the taw as either `tihyeh` ("you shall live") or `tamuth` ("you shall die") depending on the bearer's state.
- Patristic: Jerome explicitly connects the Ezekiel taw to the cross (reference ANF/NPNF — verify exact letter; commonly cited as Jerome's commentary on Ezekiel or a letter to Marcella).
- Reformed: Calvin on Ezek 9 (PD).
- Puritan: Henry on Ezek 9 (PD).

### Section 3 — Passover blood as typological anchor

Exodus 12:7, 12:13, 12:22–23 prescribe the application of blood to the lintel and the two doorposts. Some Christian tradition reads the three-position application as forming a taw/cross shape on the door (lintel top, two posts below). The text does not specify the geometry; this is a typological reading, not an exegetical deduction. Surface honestly as interpretive possibility, anchored in the strength of the pattern rather than in a geometric claim.

Heb 11:28 confirms the saving power of the blood-application without commenting on its shape. The Destroyer passes over those under the blood-mark; Israel is spared by a sign.

### Section 4 — Revelation sealing as eschatological fulfillment

Rev 7:2–3 — the angel seals 144,000 on their foreheads before the four angels release the winds of judgment. Rev 9:4 — the locusts are explicitly restricted from harming "those men which have not the seal of God in their foreheads." Rev 14:1 — the Lamb stands on Mount Zion with 144,000 bearing "his Father's name written in their foreheads."

Structural parallel to Ezekiel 9: a mark on the forehead spares the bearer from the judgment that falls on everyone else. The seal is protection; the seal is identification; the seal is covenant-mark.

### Section 5 — The cross as final taw (founder editorial slot)

The patristic tradition read the cross as the fulfillment of the taw-typology:
- **Epistle of Barnabas 9:8** (PD Apostolic Fathers) — explicit: the cross is shown in the letter Tau.
- **Tertullian, Against Marcion 3.22** (PD ANF) — the Greek letter Tau is our Latin T in shape, and this shape he foresaw as the cross.
- **Jerome** — direct connection of the Ezekiel 9:4 mark to the cross (verify PD source).

This framework section is the page's interpretive climax. The founder editorial slot (per §4.3) belongs here — Pastor Marc frames the typological closure: the instrument of Christ's death takes the shape of the letter that meant "mark," "sign," "seal," and "spared." The pattern resolves in a body on a piece of wood.

## 10. Known contested points (surface honestly)

1. **Passover blood geometry.** The claim that the three blood-positions form a taw-shape is typological reading, not textual. Ex 12 specifies where the blood goes but not what shape it makes. Surface as interpretive possibility with strength drawn from the larger pattern convergence — do not overstate as exegetical fact.

2. **Ezekiel's readers' Christological awareness.** The typological reading of Ezek 9:4 as Christological prefigurement is retrospective (NT-lit). Ezekiel's original audience would have read the taw as covenant-mark in its immediate Babylonian-siege context. Do not claim first-century Jews "already knew" the taw prefigured the cross. The typological reading is the church's reading, not the prophet's self-consciousness.

3. **Paleo-Hebrew ubiquity at Ezekiel's time.** By Ezekiel's era (6th c. BC), paleo-Hebrew was giving way to Aramaic square script among the exilic community. The letter Ezekiel envisions is the paleo-Hebrew form, which supports the reading; but the script transition is mid-flight in this period. Frame with appropriate historical care.

4. **Jerome citation.** Well-attested in the patristic tradition; exact letter/commentary citation needs verification against PD sources. If unverifiable to a specific passage, paraphrase the tradition rather than invent.
