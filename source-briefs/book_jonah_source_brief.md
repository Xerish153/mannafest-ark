## 1. Book identity

Jonah is the OT's most humane prophetic book — a narrative about a prophet (not a collection of his oracles) who runs from God's call, is swallowed by a great fish, relents, preaches to Nineveh, watches the enemy city repent, and is unable to forgive God for the mercy he himself received. The last chapter closes with God's question to Jonah about the gourd and the city, unanswered in the text.

The book's theological center is Jonah 4:2 — Jonah's bitter confession that he ran not because he feared God's wrath but because he feared God's mercy: "I knew that thou art a gracious God, and merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness, and repentest thee of the evil." This is Ex 34:6–7 in Jonah's mouth, applied by him as accusation. The book weaponizes Israel's own confession of God's covenantal character against the prophet who thought it belonged only to his people.

Jesus twice cites Jonah as a type of himself — "as Jonas was three days and three nights in the whale's belly; so shall the Son of man be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth" (Matt 12:40) and the "sign of Jonas" (Luke 11:29–32).

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Jonah 4:2 is the book's theological center — Jonah explicitly naming God's covenantal hesed as the reason he fled. Anchor of the book's reversal: the prophet is the unrepentant one, the pagans repent. Captures the book's moral force without the fish-narrative distraction.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** Jonah (traditional; the book's narrator is third-person but names Jonah as subject); alternatively attributed to an anonymous post-exilic writer in some critical readings
- **Date:** disputed — ministry itself c. 793–753 BC (reign of Jeroboam II; Jonah is named in 2 Kgs 14:25 as prophesying to that king). Composition date varies in modern scholarship.
- **Audience:** Israel, by way of a narrative about a prophet sent to Nineveh
- **Theme:** God's mercy reaches the enemy
- **Distinguishing notes:** narrative-prophet book (unique among OT prophets); closes with unanswered divine question

## 9. Known contested points

- **Historicity.** Is Jonah's fish-narrative historical or parabolic/allegorical? Traditional view: historical. Some modern readings (including within evangelical scholarship): parabolic. Jesus' "sign of Jonah" citation is read either as affirming historicity (traditional) or as argumentum-ad-hominem from an accepted reference point (minority evangelical + mainline critical). §7.9 discipline — present both without adjudicating.
- **The "great fish."** Species speculation (whale, shark, special-creation marine creature) is pastorally interesting but textually underdetermined. Surface briefly; don't speculate.
- **Nineveh's repentance extent.** Historically, Nineveh did decline and fall (c. 612 BC per Nahum) — how to reconcile Jonah's repentance with Nahum's judgment. Most resolutions: temporary repentance with later relapse. Surface.
- **The book's author.** Third-person narrative about Jonah, not necessarily by Jonah. Traditional view: Jonah. Alternative: anonymous later writer using Jonah as literary subject. Note; don't dwell.

## 10. Non-goals
