## 1. Book identity

Micah prophesies in 8th century BC Judah, contemporary with Isaiah but from a village (Moresheth) rather than the capital. His concern is for the peasantry crushed by the wealthy's land-grabs, and his judgment against unjust leaders is unflinching. The book alternates judgment and hope in three cycles: doom (chs 1–2) and salvation (chs 2:12–13), doom (ch 3) and salvation (chs 4–5), doom (chs 6–7:6) and salvation (ch 7:7–20).

Micah carries two of the OT's most-cited messianic prophecies. Mic 5:2 names Bethlehem Ephratah as the Messiah's birthplace — quoted by the chief priests in Matt 2:6 to Herod. Mic 6:8 gives perhaps the most comprehensive single-verse summary of OT ethics: "to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with thy God." Both verses carry NT-scale weight.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Mic 6:8 is the OT's most memorable one-verse ethical summary. Captures the prophetic call in three obligations. Preferred over Mic 5:2 (which is more specifically predictive) for signature placement because 6:8 speaks to all readers across all periods.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** Micah of Moresheth-Gath
- **Date:** c. 735–700 BC (reigns of Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah — per Mic 1:1)
- **Audience:** Judah and Israel; Jerusalem and Samaria
- **Theme:** God's lawsuit against his people — and his enduring covenant love
- **Distinguishing notes:** country-prophet voice (from Moresheth, not Jerusalem); three-cycle structure of doom and hope; two major messianic prophecies

## 9. Known contested points

- **Mic 4:1–3 vs. Isa 2:2–4.** Near-identical passages. Literary priority debated. Most conservative scholarship: common oracle quoted by both; others argue one depends on the other. Note; don't adjudicate.
- **Mic 5:2 fulfillment.** Birthplace prophecy fulfilled in Jesus (Matt 2:1) is universally accepted within Christian tradition. The "goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting" clause is read Christologically in traditional reading; some modern critical scholarship reads it as royal hyperbole about the Davidic dynasty.
- **Mic 7:7–20 speaker identity.** Whether the closing confession is Micah's personal voice, Israel personified, or a later liturgical addition is debated in technical scholarship. For a book hub, note briefly; don't dwell.
