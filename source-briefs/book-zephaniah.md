## 1. Book identity

Zephaniah prophesies during Josiah's early reign (c. 640–622 BC) — before the king's great reform — carrying one of the most unflinching Day-of-the-LORD visions in the Minor Prophets and one of its tenderest restoration passages. The book moves from universal judgment (1:2–3), to Judah-specific indictment (1:4–2:3), to oracles against surrounding nations (2:4–15), to Jerusalem's judgment (3:1–8), to the book's closing restoration promise (3:9–20) where God rejoices over his people "with singing" (3:17).

Zephaniah's opening (1:14–18, "the great day of the LORD is near") gave the medieval hymn *Dies Irae* its first line — "dies irae, dies illa." Yet Zeph 3:17 — "he will rejoice over thee with joy; he will rest in his love, he will joy over thee with singing" — is perhaps the OT's most intimate picture of divine affection. Both readings are the same God in the same book; the prophet will not let either go.

Zephaniah's opening genealogy (1:1) traces him back four generations to Hezekiah — uniquely in the prophetic corpus — likely King Hezekiah, making Zephaniah of royal Davidic lineage.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Zeph 3:17 is the book's tender counterweight to the Day-of-the-LORD chapters. Short, intimate, often-preached. Unique in the OT for the image of God *singing* over his people.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** Zephaniah, son of Cushi, descendant of Hezekiah (likely King Hezekiah, making Zephaniah royal lineage)
- **Date:** c. 640–622 BC (Josiah's early reign, before the great reform of 622 BC)
- **Audience:** Judah and Jerusalem
- **Theme:** universal Day of the LORD and the remnant of grace
- **Distinguishing notes:** longest prophetic genealogy (four generations, 1:1); royal Davidic lineage; *Dies Irae* origin in 1:14–18; striking tenderness of 3:17

## 5. Chapter summary guidance

- Ch 1: Day of the LORD — universal judgment imagery; Judah's specific idolatry (Baal, star-worship, Milcom); the great day is "near, and hasteth greatly" (1:14). Flag *Dies Irae* origin.
- Ch 2: call to the meek to seek the LORD (2:3); oracles against Philistia, Moab, Ammon, Cush, Assyria/Nineveh. The nations judgment predates Nahum's Nineveh judgment slightly.
- Ch 3: Jerusalem's unrepentance (3:1–7); decision to wait ("therefore wait ye upon me," 3:8); restoration (3:9–20) with the LORD in her midst singing (3:17).

## 9. Known contested points

- **Zeph 1:1 genealogy.** Is "Hizkiah" in v 1 King Hezekiah or a different Hizkiah? Tradition and most conservative commentary say King Hezekiah; modern critical scholarship often reads a non-royal namesake. Surface; don't adjudicate.
- **Exact dating within 640–622 BC.** Before Josiah's reform is near-consensus; narrower dating debated.
- **Zeph 3:9 "pure language" scope.** Read by some as restoration of pre-Babel linguistic unity; by others as eschatological worship language; by others as moral purity of speech. Note the range.
