## 1. Book identity

2 Peter is Peter's testamentary letter — written "knowing that shortly I must put off this my tabernacle" (1:14) in what the apostle signals as a farewell. The date, if authentic, is c. 65–68 AD, immediately before Peter's martyrdom. The letter addresses Christians facing two threats: internal false teachers (ch 2) and external skeptics who mock the delay of Christ's return (ch 3). Peter responds with an appeal to apostolic eyewitness — "we were eyewitnesses of his majesty" at the Transfiguration (1:16–18) — and a theology of the Day of the LORD that resists both counterfeits.

The letter contains some of the NT's most vivid eschatology. 2 Pet 3:8 ("one day is with the LORD as a thousand years, and a thousand years as one day") is widely-cited on divine time. 2 Pet 3:9 — "the LORD is not slack concerning his promise ... not willing that any should perish" — frames the delay as mercy. 2 Pet 3:10–13 describes the Day of the LORD with cosmic dissolution imagery ("the heavens shall pass away with a great noise, and the elements shall melt with fervent heat") and closes with "new heavens and a new earth, wherein dwelleth righteousness" — a direct echo of Isa 65:17.

Chapter 2 on false teachers parallels Jude 4–18 so extensively that literary dependence is near-certain in one direction or the other; Peter's priority vs. Jude's priority is debated.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

2 Pet 3:9 carries the letter's pastoral theology — God's apparent delay as mercy that humans would repent. Widely-cited; short; captures the reframing-of-delay that anchors ch 3.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** Peter the apostle (traditional; authorship is the most-contested NT authorship question)
- **Date:** if authentic, c. 65–68 AD (Peter's last letter before martyrdom); critical scholarship dates to early 2nd c. AD
- **Audience:** the same audience as 1 Peter (3:1, "this second epistle ... I now write unto you")
- **Theme:** farewell charge, warning against false teachers, the certainty of the Day of the LORD
- **Distinguishing notes:** testamentary form; chapter 2's heavy parallel with Jude; explicit eyewitness claim (1:16–18); new-heavens-and-earth (3:13) as bookend to Isa 65:17

## 9. Known contested points

- **Authorship.** Most-contested NT authorship question. Traditional: Peter, dictated or written c. 65–68 AD. Critical: pseudepigraphon written later in Peter's name. Arguments for pseudonymity: stylistic differences from 1 Peter, late attestation (Eusebius notes disputed status), echoes of Jude suggesting post-Jude dependence, apocalyptic language that may reflect later 1st-c. setting. Arguments for authenticity: apostolic testamentary genre common in Jewish/Christian antiquity, 1:14 self-identification, early circulation attested by Clement of Alexandria. §7.9 discipline — site presents traditional authorship while honestly surfacing the question.
- **Relationship to Jude.** Literary priority (Peter quotes Jude, or Jude quotes Peter, or both quote a common source) debated. Most modern scholarship: Jude prior, 2 Peter later (Jude's specificity about Enoch and Michael would be naturally omitted if Peter sanitized; harder to explain if Jude expanded Peter). Traditional: 2 Peter prior. Note; don't adjudicate.
- **3:10 textual variant.** "The earth and the works therein shall be burned up" (KJV/TR) vs. "shall be found/exposed" (eurēthēsetai, earliest manuscripts including Sinaiticus and Vaticanus). Textual-critical note; can affect eschatological reading. Present traditional KJV reading as primary; note textual variant.
- **New-heavens-and-earth scope.** Renewal-of-current-cosmos vs. replacement-of-current-cosmos. Reformed restorationist reading vs. annihilationist reading vs. continuity-with-transformation reading. Surface the range.
