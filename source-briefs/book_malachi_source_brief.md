## 1. Book identity

Malachi is the last prophetic voice of the Old Testament — a c. 430 BC oracle to the post-exilic community whose Temple has stood for roughly ninety years but whose zeal has cooled into cynical religious routine. Priests are offering blemished sacrifices; husbands are divorcing their wives; tithes are withheld; worshippers complain that service to God is profitless. Malachi confronts each symptom by a distinctive rhetorical device: six disputations in which God makes a claim, the people contest it, and God answers. The back-and-forth gives the book an unusually conversational texture among the prophets.

The book closes the OT canon with two forward-looking prophecies: a messenger to prepare the way of the LORD (Mal 3:1) and Elijah to come before the great day (Mal 4:5–6). The NT opens by fulfilling both — John the Baptist, "the voice of one crying in the wilderness" (quoted directly from Isaiah but backed by Mal 3:1 and 4:5–6; Matt 11:10 applies Mal 3:1 explicitly to John; Matt 17:10–13 applies Mal 4:5 explicitly to John; Luke 1:17 tells us John comes "in the spirit and power of Elias"). Between Malachi and John the Baptist stretch the so-called "400 silent years" — no canonical prophetic voice from Malachi's close to John's arrival at the Jordan.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Mal 3:6 — "For I am the LORD, I change not; therefore ye sons of Jacob are not consumed" — is the book's theological center. God's immutability is the ground of covenantal survival. Short, quotable, classical.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** Malachi ("my messenger"); possibly a proper name, possibly a title (some Jewish tradition reads 3:1's "my messenger" as author self-reference, treating "Malachi" as title rather than name)
- **Date:** c. 430 BC (contemporary with Nehemiah's second term; social conditions described match Neh 13)
- **Audience:** post-exilic Judah
- **Theme:** covenant faithfulness when the ordinary has lost its fire
- **Distinguishing notes:** last OT prophet; six disputations as structural device; two-part NT-prophetic close (messenger, Elijah); 400-year silence follows

## 5. Chapter summary guidance

- Ch 1: first disputation — "I have loved you" vs. "wherein hast thou loved us?" (Esau/Jacob contrast, 1:2–5); second disputation — priestly despising of God's name in blemished sacrifice (1:6–2:9).
- Ch 2: third disputation — faithlessness in marriage and divorce (2:10–16); fourth disputation — "where is the God of judgment?" (2:17).
- Ch 3: **Mal 3:1 messenger prophecy** — flag prominently; fifth disputation — robbing God in tithes and offerings (3:6–12); sixth disputation — speaking against God (3:13–15). Note 3:16–18 remnant response ("a book of remembrance was written").
- Ch 4: Day of the LORD announcement; sun of righteousness with healing in wings (4:2); **Mal 4:5–6 Elijah prophecy** — flag prominently; fulfilled in John the Baptist per Matt 17:10–13.

## 9. Known contested points

- **"Malachi" — proper name or title.** Talmudic tradition (Megillah 15a) proposes Ezra as the author, treating "Malachi" as title. Christian tradition treats as proper name. Pushes no critical weight either way; note.
- **Mal 2:16 "I hate divorce" reading.** KJV: "The LORD, the God of Israel, saith that he hateth putting away." Modern translations sometimes read as describing a man who hates his wife and divorces, reversing the sense. Textual-critical question. Note; don't adjudicate.
- **Elijah's identity (4:5–6).** Traditional Christian reading: John the Baptist per the NT's apostolic interpretation, which is settled within Christian tradition. Some dispensational readings hold additionally for a future literal Elijah return before the Second Coming. Note.
- **Canon order.** In the Hebrew Bible, Chronicles closes the canon, not Malachi. Christian order closes with Malachi (deliberately, to bridge directly to the Gospels). Structural observation, not a polemic.
