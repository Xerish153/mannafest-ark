## 1. Book identity

Hosea's ministry spans the final decades of the northern kingdom (c. 755–722 BC), from prosperity under Jeroboam II through Assyrian conquest. God commands Hosea to marry Gomer — a woman who proves unfaithful — and to keep loving her even as she runs from the marriage. The marriage becomes a living parable: Israel is the unfaithful bride, YHWH the faithful husband who will not let her go.

The book moves between narrative chapters (Hosea's own family story, chs 1–3) and oracles of judgment and mercy toward Israel (chs 4–14). Hosea 6:6 ("I desired mercy, and not sacrifice") is quoted twice by Jesus (Matt 9:13, 12:7) and becomes a NT touchstone for the priority of inner faithfulness over outer ritual. Hosea 11:1 ("out of Egypt have I called my son") is quoted in Matt 2:15 as fulfilled typologically in Jesus' return from Egypt — one of the NT's most-discussed typological fulfillments.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Hos 6:6 is the theological anchor Jesus himself twice quotes. Short enough to render in full. Captures the prophet's central concern: mercy and knowledge of God over sacrifice and burnt offerings.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** Hosea, son of Beeri
- **Date:** c. 755–722 BC (ministry spans reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah of Judah; Jeroboam II of Israel and his successors)
- **Audience:** the northern kingdom of Israel in its final generation
- **Theme:** covenant love that will not let go
- **Distinguishing notes:** only prophet whose personal marriage becomes the book's central parable; mixes narrative and oracle

## 9. Known contested points

- **Gomer's status at marriage** — was she already a prostitute or became so? Hos 1:2 reads either way. Surface both readings briefly; don't adjudicate.
- **Matt 2:15 typology** — Matthew applies Hos 11:1 (originally about the Exodus) to Jesus. Some see this as legitimate typology (Israel and Jesus as parallel "sons"); critical scholars call it eisegesis. §7.9 discipline.
