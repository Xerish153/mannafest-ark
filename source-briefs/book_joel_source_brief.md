## 1. Book identity

Joel interprets a devastating locust plague as a foretaste of the Day of the LORD and calls Judah to corporate repentance. In chapter 2, the prophet moves from the locust crisis to a promise of restoration: God will "restore the years the locust has eaten" and — climactically — pour out his Spirit on all flesh, sons and daughters prophesying, old men dreaming, young men seeing visions. Peter quotes this passage at Pentecost (Acts 2:16–21) as inaugurated-fulfilled in the apostolic outpouring.

Joel's brevity (three chapters) belies its theological weight. The Spirit-outpouring promise, the Day of the LORD framework, and the eschatological closing vision of the LORD's kingdom in Zion all make the book Scripture-wide in influence despite its short length.

## 2. Signature verse rationale

Joel 2:28–29 is the Pentecost prophecy — Peter's chosen OT anchor for interpreting the Acts 2 outpouring. Inaugurated fulfillment at Pentecost; progressive fulfillment through church history; consummation anticipated per Vision §4.6 tri-fold frame.

## 3. Stat strip

- **Author:** Joel, son of Pethuel
- **Date:** disputed — pre-exilic (c. 835 BC, Jehoash's minority) and post-exilic (c. 500–400 BC) are both proposed. Internal evidence ambiguous; site default notes both without adjudicating.
- **Audience:** Judah and Jerusalem
- **Theme:** the Day of the LORD and the Spirit poured out
- **Distinguishing notes:** uniquely difficult to date; Pentecost fulfillment makes the Spirit-outpouring passage NT-foundational

## 9. Known contested points

- **Dating.** Ninth century (pre-exilic, with Joash's minority as the "elders" guiding) vs. fifth century (post-exilic, Second Temple reconstruction era). Both have PD-citable defenders. Surface; don't adjudicate.
- **Locust vs. human army (ch 2).** Whether ch 2's "great people and strong" (2:2) is the locusts extended into poetic military imagery or a literal coming human army — both readings present.
- **Pentecost fulfillment — completed or inaugurated?** Most Reformed readings take Pentecost as inaugurated-fulfillment (the Spirit outpoured, but eschatological consummation awaits). Some dispensational readings hold the full prophecy awaits. §7.9 discipline.
