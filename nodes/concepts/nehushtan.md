# Nehushtan

The mocking name King Hezekiah gave to Moses' bronze serpent (Numbers 21:8–9) when he broke it during his religious reforms, c. 715 BC. From Hebrew **nehoshet** (נְחֹשֶׁת, bronze) — literally *"the bronze thing"* — stripping the object of any sacred aura and framing it for what it had become: an idol.

The 2 Kings 18:4 note is one of the most theologically consequential single verses in the Old Testament. For approximately seven centuries between Moses and Hezekiah, the bronze serpent — a divinely-appointed symbol of atonement — was preserved through the conquest, judges, and monarchy. By Hezekiah's reign, Israelites were burning incense to it (2 Kgs 18:4). Hezekiah breaks it as part of his larger idolatry purge.

The principle: **a divinely-appointed symbol can become an idol when the symbol eclipses the reality it points to.** The modern application is uncomfortable but direct — the cross itself is subject to the same danger.

**Talmud Chullin 6b** preserves a Jewish tradition that earlier reforming kings Asa and Jehoshaphat deliberately left the bronze serpent alone so Hezekiah would have something worthy to break. The Jewish tradition picks up the same theological beat the Christian reading sees.
