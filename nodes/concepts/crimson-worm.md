# The Crimson Worm — Tola'at Shani

Hebrew **tola'at** (תּוֹלַעַת) is not the generic Hebrew word for *worm*. It refers specifically to **Kermes vermilio** (older classification: *Coccus ilicis*) — the crimson-dye worm that produces the scarlet pigment used in the Tabernacle textiles (the "scarlet" of Ex 25:4, 26:1, 28:5, and elsewhere).

The entomological detail is load-bearing: when the female crimson worm is ready to lay eggs, she climbs a tree (commonly the kermes oak), affixes herself to the wood, and dies as her young consume her body. Her scarlet body stains the tree red; the hatchlings emerge from beneath the dye-soaked corpse three days later; the pigment is then harvested for the crimson dye known in Hebrew as **shani** (שָׁנִי).

In **Psalm 22:6** — the Messianic lament — the speaker says *"I am a worm, and no man."* The Hebrew word is **tola'at**, not the generic *rimmah*. The specific worm chosen is the one whose death stains the wood scarlet and whose young are born of her death.

Christ quotes **Psalm 22:1** from the cross (Matthew 27:46 / Mark 15:34 — *Eli, Eli, lama sabachthani*). Pinned to wood. Stained scarlet. Rising three days later.
