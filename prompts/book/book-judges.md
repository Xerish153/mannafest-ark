## Identity

The post-conquest slide into cyclical apostasy. Twelve judges delivering Israel from successive oppressors; a sin → servitude → supplication → salvation pattern recurring seven times; an appendix whose moral violence is the book's real diagnosis. "In those days there was no king in Israel" closes the book — an argument, not a lament.

## Framework sections (depth 2)

- The deuteronomic cycle — the book's structuring logic. Henry on the cycle as a parable of the human heart; Calvin on its theological necessity.
- Judges as proto-prophets — each judge delivers and proclaims; Spirit-empowered deliverance (3:10, 6:34, 11:29, 13:25, 14:6).
- Gideon and Samson as contrasting types — bold reluctance and its post-deliverance slide; Nazirite national-type.
- The appendix (17–21) as diagnosis — Micah's household idolatry + the Levite's concubine + Benjaminite civil war. Thematic climax, not chronological late addition.
